## Preface: What This Is

This is not a utopian manifesto. It is an engineering document grounded in a theological one.

The Constitution for Decentralized Meaning, appended in full at the end of this playbook, provides the epistemic and moral framework within which all practical decisions in this guide are made. It is not decoration. It is load-bearing. Where the playbook tells you *what* to do, the Constitution explains *why* — and more importantly, names the failure modes that have destroyed every community that attempted this before us.

The thesis is simple: a community of 150 people on approximately 850 acres of good land, operating with genuine mutual care, low-to-moderate technology, and a shared orientation toward the incarnate Logos, can meet all of its material needs with roughly 2–3 hours of daily labour per person, leaving the remainder of human life for everything that makes it worth living.

This has been done before. Many times. In many cultures. It has also failed before — not through crop failure or bad weather, but through the specific, named failure modes the Constitution identifies: the replacement of Christ by institution, the substitution of growth for fruit, the criminalisation of dissent, the accumulation of power without exposure to cost.

We do not assume we are immune to those failures. We assume we are exactly as susceptible as everyone who came before us. The Constitution is the structure we build in advance of the failure, not in response to it.

**This document assembles the practical knowledge. The Constitution holds the frame.**

## The Two Documents, One Framework

Before reading any further, understand the relationship between these two texts:

The **Playbook** addresses the material and organisational questions: how much land, what crops, which tools, what governance structures, how to preserve food, how to train a midwife.

The **Constitution** addresses the deeper questions that determine whether any of the practical answers matter: What is this community answerable to? How does it correct itself? How does it prevent its own authority from becoming the thing it was built to resist?

The Constitution's closing line is the best summary of their relationship:

*"This constitution is not the fire. It is the hearth."*

This playbook is the building in which the hearth sits. Neither is sufficient alone.

## Part One: Foundations

### 1.1 The Epistemic Ground

*Constitution, Article I: Truth is grounded in the person of Jesus Christ, not in doctrine alone, not in institution, and not in consensus. All claims of meaning, authority, or interpretation remain answerable to fidelity to Christ, measured by resemblance in life, love, sacrifice, and truthfulness under cost.*

This community is not built on an idea, a political philosophy, a utopian vision, or the personality of its founders. It is built on a person — and on the claim that life oriented toward that person, genuinely and without performance, produces a specific quality of community that can be recognised, tested, and if necessary corrected.

This means:
- No founder, elder, or council member speaks with final authority
- Every decision is answerable to a question: does this resemble him?
- The community's fruit — not its growth, not its efficiency, not its happiness — is the measure of its fidelity

The practical implication is that the structures in this document are provisional. They are our best current understanding of how to organise 150 people well. They are answerable to better understanding and to lived experience. They are not sacred. He is sacred. These are just arrangements.

### 1.2 The Core Numbers

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Target population | 150 people | Dunbar's number — maximum trust density |
| Total land required | 750–900 acres | ~1 acre per person plus commons |
| Arable land | ~500 acres | Crops, rotation, legumes |
| Pasture and fodder | ~175 acres | Animal keeping |
| Forest and woodland | ~150 acres | Fuel, timber, forage, medicine |
| Settlement footprint | ~25 acres | Buildings, paths, gardens, commons |
| Daily labour per person | ~2–3 hours | Assuming full cooperation |
| Maximum community diameter | ~1.4 miles | Everyone within 15-minute walk |
| Neighbour spacing | ~200 feet | Within shouting distance |

The 150-person limit is not arbitrary. It reflects the maximum scale at which human beings can hold each other in genuine mutual awareness — where every person is within overlapping sight and sound of multiple neighbours, where nobody is invisible, where care is ambient rather than institutional. The geometry of the village at this scale creates the conditions for the ethics. Scale past it and you need systems to replicate what proximity once provided for free. We do not scale past it.

### 1.3 The Mutual Visibility Principle

At 150 people on this land area, every person is within overlapping awareness of multiple neighbours at all times. This is not surveillance. It is presence. Nobody falls through because nobody is invisible.

*Constitution, Article II: Because Logos became flesh, truth is publicly observable, morally legible, and embodied. Knowledge of truth is not limited to elites, experts, or institutions.*

The Incarnation establishes a principle that the village enacts physically: truth is visible, embodied, and accessible to anyone paying attention. The neighbour whose fire is not lit by morning is already a signal before anyone has decided whether to respond to it. The community member who is withdrawing, struggling, or drifting is legible before the condition becomes a crisis.

This mutual visibility is the community's primary care infrastructure. It is more sensitive than any formal system we could build. It must be protected — which means: resist any architectural or social arrangement that reduces it. Private compounds. Factions. Unspoken hierarchies. Anything that makes people less visible to each other is a degradation of the community's immune system.

### 1.4 The Technology Filter

Before adopting any technology, apply this test:

**Does this give us more life, or does it just give us more?**

Secondary questions:
- Can it be maintained locally, by people we train ourselves?
- Does it require ongoing external supply chains?
- Does it pull people apart or push them together?
- If it broke tomorrow and could never be replaced, would we be worse off than before we had it?

*Constitution, Article V: The accumulation of power without proportional exposure to cost constitutes drift from fidelity.*

This principle applies to technology as much as to governance. A technology that gives one person or group disproportionate power over others — through control of a critical system, through specialised knowledge that cannot be distributed, through dependency it creates — is a governance risk dressed as a practical solution. Apply the same scrutiny to tools as to titles.

Technologies that pass: hand tools, optical glass, printed books, fermentation, passive solar design, bicycles, gravity-fed water systems, wind and water mills, germ theory and basic medicine, seed selection knowledge, radio receivers.

Technologies that fail: anything whose maintenance requires the global industrial system that built it, or that concentrates irreplaceable power in fewer than three people.

## Part Two: Site Selection

### 2.1 Non-Negotiable Criteria

**Water.** A reliable, clean, gravity-feedable water source is the single most important site criterion. A spring or stream above the settlement elevation eliminates the need for pumping infrastructure. Minimum flow: 5 gallons per person per day for drinking and cooking (750 gpd), plus irrigation access.

**Soil.** Minimum viable: loamy soil with pH 6.0–7.5, workable with hand tools, capable of sustaining grain yields of 500+ lbs/acre without synthetic inputs. Ideal: deep topsoil, good drainage, historic agricultural use. Conduct a soil test before committing. Look for earthworms as a simple proxy indicator.

**Climate.** Frost-free growing season of at least 120 days. Annual rainfall of 20–40 inches, or reliable irrigation source. Avoid flood plains, extreme wind exposure, and areas with historically poor harvests.

**Legal.** Clear title. Zoning that permits agricultural use and dwelling construction. No encumbrances that would force engagement with external systems on terms not of our choosing. Ideally, a single parcel rather than assembled pieces.

**Access.** Reachable but not on a main route. The community needs the ability to trade and communicate with the outside world without being absorbed by it. A secondary road within reasonable distance. Not so remote that medical emergencies become impossible to address.

*Constitution, Article VII: Exit must always be possible, never fatal, but not trivial.*

This applies to physical site selection as much as governance: the community should not be so isolated that exit becomes impossible or life-threatening. The goal is sovereignty, not captivity. A member who chooses to leave must be able to leave. A site that makes this physically impossible compromises the Constitution's most basic protection.

### 2.2 Ideal Candidate Regions

**Southern and central Portugal (Alentejo region).** Depopulating rapidly. Ancient villages built at Eden scale, standing empty. Good soil. Mild winters. Olive, cork, and grain country. Low land prices. Existing stone infrastructure often available. The village is already there. We return to it.

**Romanian and Slovak Carpathians.** Rich soil. Forest resources. Strong existing folk agricultural knowledge still living in elder populations. Low land prices. The memory of the old ways is not yet fully gone. The community would not be starting from nothing — it would be re-inhabiting a tradition.

**Interior Pacific Northwest (Oregon, Washington).** Extraordinary fertility. Long growing season. Abundant water. Good timber. Legal frameworks relatively hospitable to intentional communities.

**Scottish Highlands and Islands.** Many areas were forcibly cleared in the 18th century and remain empty. Ancient runrig field systems still visible from the air. The ghosts of Eden literally written into the landscape. A particular justice in returning.

**Southern Appalachians (USA).** Rich ecological diversity. Good rainfall. Long history of self-sufficient homesteading culture. Established networks of like-minded communities nearby.

### 2.3 Site Layout Principles

The settlement should be arranged so that:
- The water source is uphill of everything
- The latrine and waste systems are downhill and downwind
- The central commons is within a 5-minute walk of all dwellings
- The gathering place occupies the highest visible point
- Fields radiate outward from the settlement rather than the settlement sitting in the middle of fields
- A clear path exists from every dwelling to every other dwelling

Draw this out before you build anything. The geometry of daily movement shapes the quality of daily life more than almost any other single decision. A village designed so that people naturally encounter each other going about ordinary tasks is a village that builds relationship without effort. A village designed for privacy and separation is a village that must manufacture the connection it has designed away.

The gathering place on the high ground is not merely symbolic. It is the community's fixed orientation point — visible from everywhere, reminding everyone at all times of the vertical dimension of their life together.

## Part Three: Governance

*Constitution, Article V: Authority is real but always derivative. Authority exists to serve truth, not replace it.*

All governance structures in this community exist to serve the community's fidelity to its ground — not to manage it, control it, or represent it. The moment any structure begins to serve itself, it has begun the drift the Constitution names. The structures below are designed to be useful. They are not designed to be permanent or sacred.

### 3.1 The Council Structure

**Full Assembly.** All adult community members (approximately 100 people, accounting for children). Meets monthly or when called by petition. Makes decisions on: land use changes, new member admission, major expenditures, changes to core agreements. Decision method: modified consensus (90% threshold, not unanimity — unanimity enables veto abuse).

*The Full Assembly is the community's primary discernment body. Constitution, Article IV: Discernment occurs through cross-community witness, exposure to consequence, and time-tested fruit. No local interpretation may claim immunity from broader evaluation.*

**Working Council.** 7–9 elected members serving staggered 2-year terms. Handles: day-to-day coordination, dispute mediation, seasonal planning, resource allocation within established policy. Meets weekly. No member serves more than 2 consecutive terms.

*Term limits are a structural implementation of Constitution, Article V: authority increases credibility through repentance; it loses credibility through denial. No position should be held long enough for its holder to forget they are a servant.*

**Domain Leads.** Appointed by Working Council, ratified by Full Assembly. One lead per major domain. Responsible for planning and coordination within their domain. Not management — facilitation. Rotate every 3 years minimum.

**Elder Council.** Advisory only. No binding authority. Consists of community members over a threshold of years-in-community (suggest: 10+ years). Their role is memory, context, and continuity. Their counsel should be sought on major decisions but they hold no veto.

*Constitution, Article VIII: Tradition serves as memory, not command. No generation completes interpretation. The Elder Council embodies this: it carries memory without exercising command. The distinction must be actively maintained.*

### 3.2 The Named Failure Modes in Governance

*Constitution, Article IX: This framework is violated when Christ is replaced by institution, ideology, or success; when growth substitutes for fruit; when dissent is reframed as disloyalty; when certainty replaces humility; when power outpaces repentance.*

Each of these failure modes has a specific governance expression. Name them in advance:

**Institution replacing Christ.** The community's governance structures begin to be treated as the point rather than the means. Compliance with process becomes more important than the quality of outcome. The council's authority becomes self-referential.
*Signal: "We followed the proper procedure" offered as sufficient justification for a harmful decision.*

**Growth substituting for fruit.** The community measures its health by expansion — new members, more land, greater influence — rather than by the quality of life and love within it.
*Signal: Recruitment energy exceeds attention to existing members' wellbeing.*

**Dissent reframed as disloyalty.** Members who raise concerns about community direction are treated as problems to be managed rather than signals to be heard.
*Signal: "You're not being a good community member" directed at someone raising a legitimate concern.*

**Certainty replacing humility.** The community's founding vision becomes fixed doctrine that cannot be questioned. The playbook becomes scripture.
*Signal: "That's not how we do things here" offered without any examination of whether how we do things is working.*

**Power outpacing repentance.** Council members or domain leads accumulate influence without accountability. Errors are managed quietly rather than acknowledged publicly.
*Signal: Community leaders who have not visibly repented for anything in years.*

Review this list at every annual Full Assembly. Ask: which of these do we see beginning?

### 3.3 Conflict Resolution

*Constitution, Article VI: Correction occurs primarily through repentance, not overthrow. Repentance must be visible, acknowledged, and costly to power. Quiet revision without acknowledgment of harm constitutes unfaithfulness.*

**Level 1 — Direct conversation.** The parties involved attempt resolution themselves. This is the first and preferred path. Community culture should normalise direct, honest, kind communication rather than avoidance or escalation. The Constitution's commitment to visible repentance begins here: at the smallest scale, between two people, before any structure is involved.

**Level 2 — Mediation.** Any community member (not a party to the dispute) can serve as mediator by consent of both parties. No formal process. The goal is understanding, not adjudication. The mediator does not decide — they witness and reflect.

**Level 3 — Working Council.** Formal hearing with both parties, written summary of the dispute, binding recommendation. Both parties agree in advance to abide by the outcome. The Council's recommendation must be grounded in the Constitution's framework — not in precedent, not in the preferences of council members, but in resemblance to the pattern.

**Level 4 — Full Assembly.** Reserved for disputes affecting the whole community or where Level 3 has failed. Rare. The social cost of reaching this level is itself a powerful incentive to resolve things earlier.

**Departure.** In cases of genuine, sustained bad faith that cannot be resolved, the community may request departure. This must be treated as a profound failure — not a clean administrative action. The Full Assembly acknowledges it publicly. The departing member leaves with fair terms (pre-established, in writing, before anyone needs them). The community examines what it missed and what it might have done differently.

*Constitution, Article VII: Exit must remain legible as signal, not erased as preference. When someone leaves, their leaving is information. Treat it as such.*

### 3.4 Membership

**Probationary period.** New members spend 6–12 months in the community before full membership is granted. During this period they participate fully in work and life but do not vote on major decisions. Both parties can end the arrangement.

**Admission criteria.** Demonstrated capacity for sustained cooperation. Genuine alignment with the Constitution's commitments — not doctrinal agreement on every point, but shared orientation toward the ground. Practical skills the community needs. Emotional maturity and capacity for honest conflict. Not: wealth, specific theological positions beyond the Constitution's core, or the right connections.

**Children.** Children born into the community are full community members. When they reach adulthood they choose whether to remain. This is not negotiable. Communities that cannot release their children become prisons, however gentle. The Constitution's commitment to non-coercive truth (Article I: authoritative but not coercive) applies here with particular force.

**Exit.** Any member may leave at any time. Pre-agree the terms in writing before anyone needs them. A community that makes exit difficult is a community that has confused belonging with captivity.

### 3.5 The Core Agreements

These should be written, read aloud at every Full Assembly, and understood by every member. They are not the Constitution — they are the community's lived translation of it into specific commitments.

Suggested framework:

1. We are answerable to Christ, not to each other's approval, not to our own comfort, and not to this community's continuation.
2. We care for each other before ourselves, trusting that this makes each of us more secure, not less.
3. We work honestly and fully. We do not calculate whether we are getting our fair share.
4. We speak directly and kindly. We do not say behind someone's back what we would not say to their face.
5. We hold the land in trust for those who come after us. We do not mine it. We improve it.
6. We treat dissent as signal. We treat those who raise concerns as gifts, not threats.
7. We repent publicly when we cause public harm. We do not revise quietly.
8. We choose this place, these people, and this life every day. Nobody is trapped here.

## Part Four: Roles and Labour

*Constitution, Article II: No authority may claim legitimacy while insulating itself from suffering, accountability, or correction.*

This principle governs the entire domain structure. No role in this community is exempt from ordinary work. The Domain Lead for food production works in the fields. The Working Council member milks cows when needed. The person who leads worship also repairs fences. The integration of role and ordinary labour is not a management philosophy — it is a fidelity requirement. The moment a leader's role insulates them from cost, their authority begins to drift.

### 4.1 Domain Structure

**Domain 1: Food Production.** Grains, vegetables, legumes, fruit. ~30–35% of adult labour hours in peak season.

**Domain 2: Animal Husbandry.** Dairy, eggs, meat, draught animals, fibre animals. ~15% of adult labour hours year-round.

**Domain 3: Water and Sanitation.** System maintenance, monitoring, seasonal management. ~2–5% of ongoing hours but absolutely critical. Cannot be neglected or deprioritised.

**Domain 4: Building and Maintenance.** Construction, repair, tool maintenance, roads and paths. ~10–15% of adult labour hours, heavier in early years.

**Domain 5: Food Preservation and Processing.** Milling, fermentation, curing, drying, storage management. ~10% of adult labour hours, concentrated in harvest season.

**Domain 6: Health and Medicine.** Care of the sick, childbirth support, herb growing, basic surgery, mental health support. ~5–8% of adult labour hours. Requires dedicated trained individuals. Cannot be treated as residual.

**Domain 7: Education and Knowledge.** Children's education, adult skill-sharing, library curation, apprenticeship coordination. ~5% of adult labour hours plus the informal teaching embedded in all other domains.

**Domain 8: Commons and Culture.** Gathering space maintenance, celebration, music, art, storytelling, spiritual life, visitor relations, external communication. ~5% of adult labour hours.

*Constitution, Article III: Meaning is apprehended distributedly across persons, cultures, languages, and roles. Domain 8 is not a luxury domain. It is where the community's distributed meaning-making is tended. Neglect it and the community will have food but not life.*

### 4.2 Labour Accounting

The community does not pay wages. It does not keep individual accounts. But it tracks aggregate labour patterns across domains to identify imbalances — not to punish but to see clearly.

*Constitution, Article IV: Discernment occurs through cross-community witness and exposure to consequence. The community cannot discern what it cannot see. Labour tracking is a visibility tool, not a surveillance tool.*

Each domain lead estimates quarterly hours needed versus available. Full Assembly reviews quarterly. Rebalancing is a community conversation. The question is not "who is failing?" but "where are we out of balance and why?"

*Constitution, Article VII: Those closest to cost — the vulnerable, caregivers, parents, practitioners — carry privileged moral weight.*

When assessing labour fairness, weight is given not to raw hours but to type of cost. The person caring for a sick child, the midwife attending a difficult birth, the person who sat with someone through the night — these contributions are not equally visible in an hour count. The community must consciously correct for this. Invisible labour is the most common source of quiet resentment and the most common precursor to departure.

### 4.3 Skill Inventory and Training

Conduct a full skills audit of all adult members in the first year. Map current capacity against community needs. Identify critical gaps. Create apprenticeship pairings.

*Constitution, Article III: Diversity of reception is not error but design. No local interpretation may claim immunity from broader evaluation.*

This applies to skills as much as to interpretation. No skill should be held by only one person — not because of distrust, but because single-person knowledge is fragile and creates inadvertent power concentration. Three people should hold every critical skill. When only one person knows how to do something essential, that person holds disproportionate authority over everyone who depends on that knowledge. Distribute accordingly.

**Critical skills requiring minimum three holders:** Seed saving and selection. Grain milling and bread-making. Fermentation. Animal and human midwifery. Basic surgery and wound care. Herbalism. Water system maintenance. Basic carpentry. Masonry and earthen building. Blacksmithing and tool repair. Textile production. Child education. Conflict mediation.

## Part Five: Farming Guide

### 5.1 Land Allocation

| Land Use | Acres | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Grain (wheat, rye, oats, corn) | 180–200 | Includes rotation fallow |
| Legumes (beans, lentils, peas) | 80–100 | Also nitrogen-fixing |
| Vegetables and alliums | 30–40 | High labour, high nutrition |
| Fruit and nut trees | 40–50 | Long investment, long return |
| Pasture | 100–120 | Sheep, goats, cattle |
| Fodder and hay | 50–60 | Winter animal feed |
| Woodland garden | 50–60 | Managed polyculture |
| Market garden (trade surplus) | 20–30 | Optional, for external trade |

### 5.2 The Three-Field Rotation System

Divide arable land into three roughly equal blocks and rotate annually:

**Year 1 — Winter grain.** Wheat or rye. Planted in autumn, harvested following summer. Highest calorie yield per acre.

**Year 2 — Spring grain and legumes.** Oats or barley plus beans or peas intercropped or in strips. The legumes fix atmospheric nitrogen, partially restoring what the grain removed.

**Year 3 — Fallow.** The field rests. Graze animals on it to add manure. Plant green manure crops (clover, vetch) and turn them in. This field recovers its fertility for the next cycle.

*Constitution, Article VIII: No generation completes interpretation. Time tests meaning through consequence.*

The three-field rotation is a form of this principle applied to land: you cannot take from the same ground indefinitely without giving back. The fallow year is not waste — it is investment in all the years that follow it. Communities that abandon the rotation to increase short-term yield are mining their future. This is the agricultural expression of the Constitution's warning against certainty replacing humility.

### 5.3 Soil Management

**The core principle: feed the soil, not the plant.** A living soil with high organic matter, diverse microbial communities, and earthworm populations will grow healthy plants without external inputs. A depleted soil will not, regardless of what you add to it.

**Compost.** Every organic waste product — kitchen scraps, animal manure, crop residues, wood ash — goes back to the land through compost. Maintain three compost systems in rotation. Target: 2–4 inches of compost applied to planting beds annually.

**Animal integration.** Animals and crops are not separate systems. They are one system. Animal manure is the fertility engine. Move animals across the land in rotation — where animals have grazed and dunged, next year's crops grow better.

**Cover cropping.** Never leave soil bare. Bare soil loses moisture, erodes, and loses microbial communities. When a field is not in production, it is under a cover crop — clover, vetch, buckwheat, winter rye.

**No-dig beds.** For the vegetable garden, adopt permanent raised beds that are never walked on and never tilled. Soil structure builds over years. Sheet mulch with organic matter annually rather than digging.

### 5.4 Grain Production

**Target yields (low-tech, good management):** 700–900 lbs/acre for wheat and rye; 800–1000 lbs/acre for oats.

**Seed selection.** Each year, select seed from the best-performing plants in your fields — largest heads, latest to lodge, earliest to mature, most resistant to disease. Save 10–15% more than you need. This is patient, incremental work. It does not produce dramatic results quickly. It produces irreplaceable results over decades. It is among the most important work the community does and among the most easily deprioritised. Resist that.

**Harvest.** With scythes and hand tools, a team of 20 fit adults can harvest approximately 5 acres per day. This is community-wide work — everyone who can participate does.

**Storage.** Target: 18 months of grain in storage at all times. This is the community's buffer against one crop failure without starvation. Never let it fall below 12 months. The granary is the community's most important insurance instrument.

### 5.5 Animal Husbandry

**Recommended starting herd for 150 people:**

| Animal | Number | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Dairy cows | 10–12 | Milk, butter, cheese, occasional meat |
| Goats | 15–20 | Milk, cheese, meat, brush clearing |
| Pigs | 8–10 | Waste conversion, meat, lard |
| Chickens | 100–150 | Eggs daily, meat, insect control |
| Sheep | 20–30 | Wool, milk, meat, land management |
| Draught animals | 4–6 oxen or horses | Field work, hauling |
| Bees | 10–15 hives | Pollination, honey, wax |

**The daily reality of animals.** Animals require care every single day of the year, including holidays and illness and bad weather. This is the most scheduling-constrained domain in the community. Roster the animal care team with explicit backup coverage for every role. Never assume informal coverage.

**Breeding strategy.** Maintain sufficient breeding stock to be fully self-sufficient in animal reproduction. Do not become dependent on purchasing animals from outside. The community that cannot reproduce its own animals has created a dependency it may not be able to sustain.

### 5.6 The Vegetable Garden

Target 200–300 square feet of intensive garden bed per person.

**Year-round production calendar (temperate climate):**

*Winter:* Kale, leeks, root vegetables in storage, winter salad greens under cold frames.
*Early Spring:* Broad beans, peas, lettuce, spinach, overwintered onions.
*Late Spring:* Transplant tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers, squash. Direct sow carrots, beetroot, more beans.
*Summer:* Peak production. Harvest continuously. Begin preservation immediately.
*Autumn:* Root vegetable harvest and storage. Brassicas. Last tomatoes. Begin winter preparations.

Preservation must keep pace with production. A garden that produces more than can be preserved is not a functioning system. Plan preservation capacity before planting, not after harvest.

### 5.7 Orchard and Woodland

Plant the orchard in the first year. The orchard is the community's longest investment and its most patient return. It asks the founders to work for the people who come after them. This is the material form of the Constitution's commitment to serving something beyond the present generation.

**Recommended mix (temperate climate):** Apples and pears (cider and eating varieties). Plums and damsons. Chestnuts (high-calorie, stores well, partly substitutes grain). Hazelnuts. Walnuts. Mulberries. Elderberry and elderflower. Quince.

**Forest garden.** Establish a managed woodland garden with multiple layers: canopy trees, understory fruit and nut trees, shrubs, ground layer herbs and vegetables, climbers, root layer. Once established, this system produces food with minimal intervention and builds soil over time. It is the most patient and the most enduring of the community's food systems.

## Part Six: Infrastructure

### 6.1 Water Systems

The gravity-fed system is the goal. Find the spring or stream intake point above the settlement. Lay pipes from intake to a settling tank, then to a distribution point at the settlement. From there, gravity delivers water to key points without pumping.

Key water points to serve: central village fountain (social hub, not merely utility), kitchen and food processing buildings, animal watering points, laundry and bathing facilities, garden irrigation, fire protection cistern.

**Sanitation.** Separate grey water from black water from the beginning. Grey water can irrigate non-food plants after simple settling. Black water requires composting toilets or a properly sited pit system, always downhill and downwind of water and food. Germ theory tells you everything you need: get human waste away from water and food. The specific technology matters less than the principle, consistently applied.

### 6.2 Buildings

**Design for climate.** The single most important building design principle is passive climate response.

**Passive solar design basics:**
- Orient main living spaces to face the sun (south in northern hemisphere, north in southern)
- Large windows on the sun-facing side; small windows on the shade side
- Thermal mass (stone, brick, earth) inside the sun-facing glass to absorb and release heat
- Deep roof overhangs to shade in summer, allow winter sun
- Good insulation in roof and walls
- Earth sheltering on the cold side where possible

**Building materials.** Use what is local and abundant: stone, timber, earth (cob, adobe, rammed earth), lime plaster. These materials are repairable, recyclable, and do not require global supply chains.

*Constitution, Article II: No authority may claim legitimacy while insulating itself from suffering, accountability, or correction.*

Apply this principle to buildings: the commons buildings should be the best-built structures in the community. The meeting hall, the kitchen, the school, the medical room, the library. Private dwellings can be simple. Shared spaces should be beautiful. The community that builds lavish private homes and functional commons has already made a statement about what it values.

### 6.3 Energy

**Primary heat: wood.** Managed coppice woodland provides sustainable wood fuel indefinitely. Coppicing produces poles and fuel on 7–20 year rotation. Hazel, ash, willow, and sweet chestnut are classic coppice species. With well-designed rocket mass heaters or masonry stoves, wood consumption per household is a fraction of conventional wood burning.

**Light.** Beeswax candles and rendered tallow. Oil lamps burning pressed vegetable oil. Solar panels, if they pass the technology filter, provide battery-backed LED lighting at very low ongoing maintenance cost for 25–30 years.

**Mechanical power.** Mill grain with water or wind. Use animal draught for heavy field work. Bicycle-powered tools for small-scale mechanical tasks.

## Part Seven: Food Preservation

Without preservation, abundance becomes scarcity seasonally. With it, the community eats well year-round from its summer peak.

### 7.1 Fermentation

Fermentation is the deepest food technology humanity possesses. It preserves, transforms, and improves the nutritional value of food simultaneously.

**Lacto-fermentation of vegetables.** Sauerkraut, kimchi, fermented beets, pickled cucumbers, fermented beans. Salt draws water from vegetables creating brine in which lactobacillus bacteria preserve the food for months. No refrigeration needed.

**Dairy fermentation.** Yoghurt, kefir, butter, and cheese transform fresh milk into products lasting days (yoghurt), weeks (hard cheese), or years (aged cheese). A cool stone cheese cave is a significant community asset.

**Grain fermentation.** Beer, ale, and cider are not luxuries — they are preserved calories, safer than water historically, sources of B vitamins, made from seasonal surpluses that store for months. A community brewery is a serious food security asset.

**Sourdough.** A living sourdough culture makes leavened bread from grain and water alone. One maintained culture, fed regularly, serves a community indefinitely. It is one of the oldest living relationships between humans and the microbiological world — a form of covenant maintained through daily attention.

### 7.2 Root Cellaring

A well-constructed root cellar maintains 32–40°F (0–4°C) with moderate humidity year-round without active refrigeration. In these conditions the following store for months: carrots, beets, turnips, parsnips, potatoes, cabbage, apples, pears, celeriac, winter squash, onions, garlic, dried beans, dried grains.

Build the root cellar generously. You will always wish you had built it larger.

### 7.3 Drying and Salting

**Drying.** Removes water from food, preventing microbial growth. Herbs, mushrooms, beans, peas, sliced fruit, and some vegetables dry in air and sun. Dried goods store for 1–3 years in cool, dry conditions.

**Salt curing.** Preserves meat and fish for months to years. Requires a reliable salt supply — factor this into site selection and trading relationships. Smoked meat combines salt, drying, and antimicrobial smoke compounds.

## Part Eight: Health and Medicine

### 8.1 Prevention First

*Constitution, Article II: Because Logos became flesh, truth is publicly observable, morally legible, and embodied.*

The Incarnation is the theological basis for taking physical bodies seriously. A community that neglects the body in favour of the spirit has misread the Gospel. Health is not a concession to material existence — it is care for the form in which persons are given to us.

The single highest-return health investment is the application of germ theory to daily life. Clean water. Proper sanitation. Hand washing. Clean birth practices. Wound cleaning. These cost essentially nothing and save more lives than any other intervention.

**The medical garden.** Maintain a dedicated garden of medicinal plants.

*Wound and infection:* Calendula, yarrow, plantain, garlic, honey, lavender.
*Fever and inflammation:* Willow bark, elderflower, peppermint.
*Respiratory:* Thyme, horehound, elderberry, mullein, elecampane.
*Digestive:* Chamomile, ginger, fennel, meadowsweet, slippery elm.
*Nervous system:* Valerian, passionflower, lemon balm, St. John's Wort.
*Women's health:* Raspberry leaf, motherwort, blue cohosh, shepherd's purse.

### 8.2 Skilled Practitioners

*Constitution, Article VII: Those closest to cost carry privileged moral weight.*

The community's health practitioners are among those closest to cost. They see suffering directly and regularly. Their counsel on community health decisions carries special weight — not because they hold authority, but because they hold proximity to consequence.

**Midwife.** Competent in normal birth, common complications, and postpartum care. Trained by an experienced practitioner, not books alone. This is the most critical medical role in the community. Childbirth complications handled routinely in hospitals are life-threatening without competent assistance.

**Wound care and basic surgery.** Knowledge of cleaning, suturing, and dressing wounds. Appendectomy technique. Bone-setting. Recognition of conditions requiring external hospital referral.

**Herbalist.** Deep knowledge of the medical garden and local wild medicinals. Preparation of tinctures, salves, teas, and poultices. Applied botanical pharmacology, not mysticism.

**Mental health support.** A community of 150 people will contain people experiencing depression, anxiety, grief, trauma, and relational difficulty. Someone needs to be trained and trusted to hold space for this.

*Constitution, Article IX: Suffering may not be spiritualised away.*

This is one of the named failure modes. A community that responds to mental distress with spiritual encouragement and nothing else has violated the Constitution's core commitment to incarnate reality. The person in the dark night needs both: someone who can pray with them and someone who can sit with them at 3am. Ideally the same person.

### 8.3 The External Medicine Line

Know what you can handle internally and what you cannot. Agree this in advance. Maintain an honest relationship with external medical services without shame or ideological resistance.

Keep a supply of medicines you cannot produce internally: antibiotics (discuss with a sympathetic doctor), epinephrine, insulin if needed, appropriate medications for chronic conditions. These are not compromise. They are the intersection of humility and care.

## Part Nine: Education and Knowledge

### 9.1 The School

*Constitution, Article III: Meaning is apprehended distributedly across persons, cultures, languages, and roles. Diversity of reception is not error but design.*

Children are not blank slates to be filled with the community's ideology. They are persons with their own reception of reality. The goal of education is not to produce faithful community members — it is to produce full human beings who have been given the tools to receive truth and the freedom to respond to it honestly.

**The goal of Eden education:**
- Full literacy and numeracy
- Deep ecological knowledge of the local land (plants, animals, seasons, weather)
- Practical skills across all domains, introduced progressively by age
- History and philosophy — why we are here, what was lost, what was chosen
- Music, drawing, storytelling — the full human range
- Critical thinking and honest questioning, including of this community and its premises
- Knowledge of the outside world — not to romanticise it, but to understand it

That last point demands emphasis. A community that educates its children in ignorance of the outside world is not protecting them — it is disabling their capacity for genuine, free choice. Constitution Article VII requires that exit remain possible. Exit is only genuinely possible for a person who understands what they would be entering.

**Who teaches.** Everyone teaches something. Formal coordination of younger children's education requires dedicated people. But the community's collective knowledge is the curriculum. Learning is integrated into life, not separated from it.

### 9.2 The Library

Print matters. Maintain a physical library of reference works that can function without any electronic infrastructure.

**Essential holdings:**

*Agriculture:* The One-Straw Revolution (Fukuoka). Farmers of Forty Centuries (King). Gaia's Garden (Hemenway). The Market Gardener (Fortier). Seed to Seed (Ashworth).

*Medicine:* Where There Is No Doctor (Werner). Where There Is No Dentist (Dickson). A Complete Herbal (Culpeper, with modern verification). Merck Manual (most recent available edition).

*Building:* The Hand-Sculpted House (Evans). Building with Earth (Norton). Timber Framing for the Rest of Us (Roy).

*Food preservation:* The Art of Fermentation (Katz). Root Cellaring (Bubel). Preserving Food Without Freezing or Canning (Terre Vivante).

*Governance and community:* Creating a Life Together (Christian). A Pattern Language (Alexander). Governing the Commons (Ostrom).

*Theology and philosophy:* The Constitution for Decentralized Meaning (this volume). The Gospel of John. Whatever speaks to the community's core. Whatever challenges it. Both are necessary.

*Constitution, Article VIII: Tradition serves as memory, not command. No generation completes interpretation.*

The library is the community's external memory. Protect it from damp, fire, and rodents. Copy deteriorating volumes by hand if necessary. The community that loses its library must rediscover what previous generations learned at cost. Do not make them pay that price again.

## Part Ten: Trade and External Relations

*Constitution, Article III: Many witnesses, one referent.*

The community is not the only witness to truth. Attempting to be is hubris, not faithfulness. Neighbouring communities, the outside world, even those who disagree with the community's premises — all carry observations the community needs. Isolation is not purity. It is epistemic poverty.

### 10.1 The Principle of Selective Engagement

The community is not an island. Attempting to be one fails. The goal is sovereignty, not isolation — the ability to engage the outside world on your own terms rather than its terms.

Engage externally for: things you genuinely cannot produce (salt, metal stock, medicine, specific seeds, books), occasional skilled services (specialist medical, legal), and the maintenance of relationships that keep you human and connected.

Do not engage externally for: things that create ongoing dependency, things that require you to produce beyond your needs in ways that distort community life.

### 10.2 What to Trade

Your surplus is your trading currency. Surpluses the community might reliably generate: high-quality preserved foods, artisan goods (cheese, fermented products, textiles, pottery, woodwork), fresh seasonal produce, seeds of locally-adapted varieties, skilled labour exchanges with neighbouring communities.

Do not trade away seed stock, breeding animals, core tools, or anything whose loss would compromise community security.

### 10.3 Neighbouring Communities as Mutual Discernment

*Constitution, Article IV: No local interpretation may claim immunity from broader evaluation. Claims that demand exemption from discernment are invalid.*

This is why neighbouring communities are not merely useful for trade and mutual aid — they are necessary for the community's spiritual health. No single community can fully discern its own drift. The village that is moving toward the failure modes in Article IX will often not see it from inside. Neighbouring communities see it from outside.

Maintain relationships with other communities that are close enough to observe you over time and honest enough to tell you what they see. This requires humility. It requires that you not treat their observations as attack when they are uncomfortable. It requires that you ask, not merely accept being told.

Formalise mutual aid agreements: in the event of crop failure, fire, illness, or other crisis, neighbouring communities provide stated support. You extend the same. This is the ancient insurance system that preceded all financial instruments.

## Part Eleven: The Spiritual Dimension

### 11.1 The Gathering Place on the Hill

*Constitution, Preamble: Truth is real but not ownable, accessible but not exhaustible, authoritative but not coercive.*

Whatever form it takes — chapel, meeting hall, stone circle, simple clearing with a view — the community needs a gathering point that is explicitly not about material life. A space where the question "what is this all for?" is not merely permitted but required.

This is not optional. Communities that neglect the meaning-making dimension of human life do not fail through crop failure. They fail through emptiness — through the slow leakage of people toward a world that offers sensation if not meaning.

The gathering place on the hill serves three functions:

**Vertical orientation.** It acknowledges that the community is embedded in something larger than itself. This acknowledgment is not weakness. It is accuracy. The Constitution grounds this in the Incarnation: the Logos became flesh, which means the material world is not separable from the spiritual. The hill, the building, the view — these are not symbols pointing away from the physical. They are physical things oriented toward what grounds all physical things.

**Weekly gathering.** Once a week, everyone comes to the same place for the same reason. Grudges are formally set aside. Deaths are mourned. Births are celebrated. The year's rhythms are marked.

*This is the Jesus OS weekly update. The community re-syncs its shared orientation. The Core Agreements are read. The named failure modes are reviewed. The community asks: are we drifting? The answer, honestly given, is the beginning of correction.*

**Beauty.** The community's most beautiful object in the most visible location. The act of building something beautiful that serves no material purpose is itself the point. It says: we do not live by bread alone. We knew exactly what bread cost. We built this anyway.

### 11.2 The Constitution as Liturgy

The Constitution for Decentralized Meaning is not merely a governance document. Read in full at the annual assembly, it functions as liturgy — as the community's formal re-articulation of what it is answerable to and what it refuses.

The reading should not be passive. After each Article, pause. Ask: where have we lived this? Where have we failed it? What do we need to repent of publicly before continuing?

*Constitution, Article VI: Repentance must be visible, acknowledged, and costly to power.*

The annual reading is the moment for this. Not a perfunctory reading but an examined one. The community that can sit with its own failure before the text it claims to live by — and name that failure aloud — is a community that is still alive.

### 11.3 Celebration and Rhythm

Mark the year. Eight festivals minimum, aligned with the agricultural and astronomical calendar: solstices, equinoxes, and the four cross-quarter days that mark the midpoints of the seasons. These are not decorative — they are the community's shared temporal framework, its sense of location in time.

Music. Story. Dance. These are not amenities. They are the technology by which communities encode values, process collective experience, and pass identity to the next generation. Maintain them with the same seriousness as the farming calendar.

*Constitution, Article III: Unity arises through convergence over time, not enforcement in advance.*

Celebration is where convergence happens naturally. The harvest feast, the midwinter gathering, the planting celebration — these are not managed unity. They are the organic expression of a community that has worked together, eaten together, mourned together, and is now marking that together. You cannot manufacture this. You can only create the conditions in which it can emerge.

## Part Twelve: Bootstrap Sequence

### Year Zero — Before You Arrive

- Read the Constitution. Not once. Many times. Until you can articulate what it forbids and why.
- Conduct full skills audit of founding members. Identify critical gaps. Fill them.
- Establish the Core Agreements. Read them aloud together. Sign them. Mean them.
- Secure land with clear title.
- Commission soil tests and water quality analysis.
- Design site layout on paper before touching ground.
- Establish root cellar and grain storage structures as first priority.
- Assemble: hand tools, seed stock (open-pollinated/heritage varieties), basic medical supplies, core library including the Constitution, fermentation equipment, basic carpentry tools.
- Identify external allies: a doctor, a veterinarian, a lawyer, an engineer sympathetic to the project.
- Name the failure modes (Constitution, Article IX) aloud as a group. Ask: which of us is most susceptible to each? This is not accusation. It is honest preparation.

### Year One — Foundations

- Build temporary shelters first. Permanent dwellings second.
- Plant the orchard immediately — it is the longest investment and the most direct act of faith in the future.
- Establish the water system before anything else.
- Plant a market garden for immediate food security while grain infrastructure is built.
- Establish composting systems.
- Begin the milking herd.
- Hold weekly assemblies. Resolve disputes early. Do not let grievances accumulate. Name them.
- Document everything. Crop yields, animal health, water quality, labour hours, disputes and their resolution.
- *At the first Full Assembly: read the Constitution aloud. Ask: have we violated any of it yet? Answer honestly.*

### Year Two — Stabilisation

- Full grain production underway.
- Begin building permanent structures systematically.
- Establish the three-field rotation.
- First preservation season — everything from summer stored for winter.
- Begin apprenticeship pairings for all critical skills.
- Establish trade relationships with neighbouring communities or towns.
- Review the Core Agreements. Amend as reality requires.
- *At the annual Full Assembly: review the Constitution's named failure modes. Which have appeared? What did we do? What will we do differently?*

### Year Three — Maturity

- Grain storage target: 18 months of supply.
- Animal herd at target population.
- Medical practitioner fully trained and practising.
- School formally operating.
- Library established.
- The community has survived at least one hard season and knows how it responds to difficulty.
- Begin considering carefully managed membership expansion.
- *First formal exchange visit with a neighbouring community for mutual discernment.*

### Year Five — Assessment

At five years, conduct a full honest assessment against two frameworks simultaneously: this playbook's practical metrics, and the Constitution's fidelity measures.

**Practical:**
- Is the land improving or degrading?
- Are people leaving? Why?
- Are children thriving?
- Is the daily labour burden sustainable long-term?

**Constitutional:**
- Is Christ still the epistemic anchor or has something else taken that role?
- Is authority still exposed to cost?
- Is dissent treated as signal?
- Has the community repented publicly for anything in the last year?
- Are the children being prepared for genuine free choice?
- What do our neighbouring communities see that we do not?

Rewrite the relevant sections of this document with what you know. Then distribute the revision to every community in your network. This is the meaning of *Constitution, Article X: amendment requires broad convergence across communities over time.*

## Appendix B: The Single Most Important Thing

Everything in this document is secondary to one thing.

**The people.**

Land can be found. Skills can be learned. Buildings can be built. Tools can be made or traded for.

What cannot be improvised is a group of 150 people who genuinely mean it — who have chosen this consciously, who understand what they are refusing, who are capable of the sustained, ordinary, daily work of caring for each other and the land without romance and without resentment.

And more specifically: 150 people who can hold their own community's structures, agreements, and authority — including this document — answerable to something beyond themselves. Who can say, together, when the moment comes: *we are wrong. We have drifted. We repent. We correct.*

## Appendix C: The Constitution for Decentralized Meaning

### A Constitution for Decentralized Meaning Grounded in the Incarnate Logos

#### Preamble

We affirm that reality is grounded in **Logos** — intelligible, personal, and relational — and that this Logos is made known not as abstraction or system, but as **incarnate life** in **Jesus**.

Therefore, truth is: real but not ownable, accessible but not exhaustible, authoritative but not coercive.

Meaning is not constructed by institutions, nor invented by consensus, but **received, witnessed, lived, and continually realigned** to the incarnate pattern revealed in Christ.

This constitution exists to: preserve meaning without monopoly, enable decentralization without relativism, allow correction without nihilism, sustain faithfulness under plural reception.

#### Article I — The Epistemic Ground

Truth is grounded in the person of Jesus Christ, not in doctrine alone, not in institution, and not in consensus. No human articulation, system, or authority exhausts the truth revealed in him. All claims of meaning, authority, or interpretation remain answerable to fidelity to Christ, measured by resemblance in life, love, sacrifice, and truthfulness under cost.

*Interpretation is always provisional; allegiance is to Christ, not to its mediators.*

#### Article II — Incarnation and Accessibility

Because Logos became flesh, truth is publicly observable, morally legible, and embodied. Knowledge of truth is not limited to elites, experts, or institutions. Truth may be apprehended through lived faithfulness, not only through formal articulation. No authority may claim legitimacy while insulating itself from suffering, accountability, or correction.

#### Article III — Pentecostal Decentralization

Meaning is apprehended distributedly across persons, cultures, languages, and roles. Diversity of reception is not error but design. Unity arises through convergence over time, not enforcement in advance. Disagreement and dissent function as signal, not threat. This decentralization is grounded not in relativism, but in shared reference to Christ. *Many witnesses, one referent.*

#### Article IV — Discernment and Community

Discernment occurs through cross-community witness, exposure to consequence, and time-tested fruit. No local interpretation may claim immunity from broader evaluation. Claims that demand exemption from discernment are invalid. Fruit is judged not by success, growth, or power, but by Christ-likeness under cost.

#### Article V — Authority and Power

Authority is real but always derivative. Authority exists to serve truth, not replace it. The accumulation of power without proportional exposure to cost constitutes drift from fidelity. Authority increases credibility through repentance; it loses credibility through denial.

#### Article VI — Error, Repentance, and Correction

Error is expected in finite interpretation. Correction occurs primarily through repentance, not overthrow. Repentance must be visible, acknowledged, and costly to power. Quiet revision without acknowledgment of harm constitutes unfaithfulness.

#### Article VII — Dissent, Exit, and Protection

Dissent must never be criminalized, suppressed, or equated with betrayal. Exit must always be possible, never fatal, but not trivial. Exit and dissent must remain legible as signal, not erased as preference. Those closest to cost — the vulnerable, caregivers, parents, practitioners — carry privileged moral weight.

#### Article VIII — Time, Memory, and Tradition

Tradition serves as memory, not command. No generation completes interpretation. Time tests meaning through consequence, but conscience may interrupt before catastrophe. No interpretation may declare itself final.

#### Article IX — Named Failure Modes

This framework is violated when: Christ is replaced by institution, ideology, or success; growth substitutes for fruit; suffering is spiritualized away; dissent is reframed as disloyalty; certainty replaces humility; power outpaces repentance. When these appear, discernment must intensify.

#### Article X — Amendment and Continuity

This constitution may be revised only through demonstrated unfaithfulness to Christ, accumulated evidence of harm, and broad convergence across communities over time. No amendment may remove: Christ as epistemic anchor, decentralization of witness, or corrigibility of authority.

#### Closing Affirmation

We do not claim to possess the truth. We claim to answer to it.

We affirm that meaning precedes procedure, reason serves fidelity, authority remains accountable, repentance is the primary mode of correction.

*This constitution is not the fire. It is the hearth. Not the Word. But a way of remaining oriented to Him without turning Him into something we control.*

**"And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us."**

This is our ground.
