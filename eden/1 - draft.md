# Project Eden: The Engineering Playbook
### A Complete Bootstrap Guide for a 150-Person Sovereign Community

---

> *"We could build this tomorrow. We have everything we need except the will to call it enough."*

---

## Preface: What This Is

This is not a utopian manifesto. It is an engineering document.

It assumes you have found — or are building — a group of people serious enough to actually do this. It assumes you understand what you are choosing and what you are refusing. It does not assume perfection. It assumes good faith, sustained effort, and enough humility to course-correct.

The thesis is simple: a community of 150 people on approximately 850 acres of good land, operating with genuine mutual care and low-to-moderate technology, can meet all of its material needs with roughly 2–3 hours of daily labour per person, leaving the remainder of human life for everything that makes it worth living.

This has been done before. Many times. In many cultures. The knowledge exists. This document assembles it.

## Part One: Foundations

### 1.1 The Core Numbers

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Target population | 150 people | Dunbar's number — maximum trust density |
| Total land required | 750–900 acres | ~1 acre per person plus commons |
| Arable land | ~500 acres | Crops, rotation, legumes |
| Pasture and fodder | ~175 acres | Animal keeping |
| Forest and woodland | ~150 acres | Fuel, timber, forage, medicine |
| Settlement footprint | ~25 acres | Buildings, paths, gardens, commons |
| Daily labour per person | ~2–3 hours | Assuming full cooperation |
| Maximum community diameter | ~1.4 miles | Everyone within 15-minute walk |
| Neighbour spacing | ~200 feet | Within shouting distance |

### 1.2 The Operating Principle

The community runs on what we will call the **Mutual Visibility Principle**: at 150 people on this land area, every person is within overlapping awareness of multiple neighbours at all times. This is not surveillance. It is presence. Nobody falls through because nobody is invisible.

This principle means:
- Care is ambient, not institutional
- Problems surface early, not late
- Resources flow where needed without bureaucratic friction
- Labour is genuinely cooperative because defection is immediately visible and socially costly — not through punishment, but through the simple fact of being known

### 1.3 The Technology Filter

Before adopting any technology, apply this test:

**Does this give us more life, or does it just give us more?**

Secondary questions:
- Can it be maintained locally, by people we train ourselves?
- Does it require ongoing external supply chains?
- Does it pull people apart or push them together?
- If it broke tomorrow and could never be replaced, would we be worse off than before we had it?

Technologies that pass: hand tools, optical glass, printed books, fermentation, passive solar design, bicycles, gravity-fed water systems, wind and water mills, germ theory and basic medicine, seed selection knowledge, radio receivers.

Technologies that fail: anything whose maintenance requires the global industrial system that built it.

## Part Two: Site Selection

### 2.1 Non-Negotiable Criteria

**Water.** A reliable, clean, gravity-feedable water source is the single most important site criterion. A spring or stream above the settlement elevation eliminates the need for pumping infrastructure entirely. Minimum flow: 5 gallons per person per day for drinking and cooking (750 gpd), plus irrigation access.

**Soil.** Minimum viable: loamy soil with pH 6.0–7.5, workable with hand tools, capable of sustaining grain yields of 500+ lbs/acre without synthetic inputs. Ideal: deep topsoil, good drainage, historic agricultural use. Conduct a soil test before committing. Look for earthworms as a simple proxy indicator.

**Climate.** Frost-free growing season of at least 120 days. Annual rainfall of 20–40 inches, or reliable irrigation source. Avoid flood plains, extreme wind exposure, and areas with historically poor harvests.

**Legal.** Clear title. Zoning that permits agricultural use and dwelling construction. No encumbrances that would force engagement with external systems (mineral rights conflicts, easements, etc.). Ideally, a single parcel rather than assembled pieces.

**Access.** Reachable but not on a main route. You want the ability to trade and communicate with the outside world without being absorbed by it. A secondary road within reasonable distance. Not so remote that medical emergencies become impossible to address.

### 2.2 Ideal Candidate Regions

**Southern and central Portugal (Alentejo region).** Depopulating rapidly. Ancient villages built at Eden scale, standing empty. Good soil. Mild winters. Olive, cork, and grain country. Low land prices. Existing stone infrastructure often available.

**Romanian and Slovak Carpathians.** Rich soil. Forest resources. Strong existing folk agricultural knowledge still living in elder populations. Low land prices. Relatively untouched by industrial agriculture.

**Interior Pacific Northwest (Oregon, Washington).** Extraordinary fertility. Long growing season. Abundant water. Good timber. Legal frameworks relatively hospitable to intentional communities.

**Scottish Highlands and Islands.** Many areas were forcibly cleared in the 18th century and remain empty. Ancient field systems still visible. A particular justice in returning. Challenging climate but manageable with good passive solar design.

**Southern Appalachians (USA).** Rich ecological diversity. Good rainfall. Long history of self-sufficient homesteading culture. Established networks of like-minded communities nearby.

### 2.3 Site Layout Principles

The settlement should be arranged so that:
- The water source is uphill of everything
- The latrine and waste systems are downhill and downwind
- The central commons is within a 5-minute walk of all dwellings
- The church, meeting hall, or equivalent gathering space occupies the highest visible point
- Fields radiate outward from the settlement rather than the settlement sitting in the middle of fields
- A clear path exists from every dwelling to every other dwelling

Draw this out before you build anything. The geometry of daily movement shapes the quality of daily life more than almost any other single decision.

## Part Three: Governance

### 3.1 The Council Structure

**Full Assembly.** All adult community members (approximately 100 people, accounting for children). Meets monthly or when called by petition. Makes decisions on: land use changes, new member admission, major expenditures, changes to core agreements. Decision method: modified consensus (90% threshold, not unanimity — unanimity enables veto abuse).

**Working Council.** 7–9 elected members serving staggered 2-year terms. Handles: day-to-day coordination, dispute mediation, seasonal planning, resource allocation within established policy. Meets weekly. No member serves more than 2 consecutive terms.

**Domain Leads.** Appointed by Working Council, ratified by Full Assembly. One lead per major domain (see Part Four). Responsible for planning and coordination within their domain. Not management — facilitation. Rotate every 3 years minimum.

**Elder Council.** Advisory only. No binding authority. Consists of community members over a threshold of years-in-community (suggest: 10+ years). Their role is memory, context, and continuity. Their counsel should be sought on major decisions but they hold no veto.

### 3.2 Conflict Resolution

**Level 1 — Direct conversation.** The parties involved attempt resolution themselves. This is the first and preferred path. Community culture should normalise direct, honest, kind communication rather than avoidance or escalation.

**Level 2 — Mediation.** Any community member (not a party to the dispute) can serve as mediator by consent of both parties. No formal process. The goal is understanding, not adjudication.

**Level 3 — Working Council.** Formal hearing with both parties, written summary of the dispute, binding recommendation. Both parties must agree in advance to abide by the outcome.

**Level 4 — Full Assembly.** Reserved for disputes that affect the whole community or that Level 3 has failed to resolve. Rare. The social cost of reaching this level is itself a powerful incentive to resolve things earlier.

**The Nuclear Option — Departure.** In cases of genuine, sustained bad faith that cannot be resolved, the community has the right to request departure. This should be treated as a profound failure and should be extraordinarily rare. The community should have pre-established fair terms for departure (housing, land share, etc.) agreed before anyone needs them.

### 3.3 Membership

**Probationary period.** New members (individual or family) spend 6–12 months in the community before full membership is granted. During this period they participate fully in work and life but do not vote on major decisions. Both parties can end the probationary period.

**Admission criteria.** The community should be clear about what it is looking for. Suggested criteria: demonstrated capacity for sustained cooperation, genuine alignment with community values, practical skills the community needs, emotional maturity and conflict resolution capacity. Not: ideological purity, wealth, specific religious belief (unless this is core to the community's identity).

**Children.** Children born into the community are full community members. When they reach adulthood they make a conscious choice whether to remain. This is non-negotiable. Communities that trap their children collapse or produce rebels.

**Exit.** Any member may leave at any time. Pre-agree the terms: what they take, what they leave, what they are owed, what they owe. Write this down before anyone needs it.

### 3.4 The Core Agreements

These should be written, read aloud at every Full Assembly, and understood by every member. Keep them short. Suggested framework:

1. We care for each other before ourselves, trusting that this makes each of us more secure, not less.
2. We work honestly and fully, not watching the clock or calculating whether we are getting our fair share.
3. We speak directly and kindly. We do not say behind someone's back what we would not say to their face.
4. We hold the land in trust for those who come after us. We do not mine it. We improve it.
5. We welcome strangers with hospitality and discernment. We are not closed, but we are not naive.
6. We choose this place, these people, and this life every day. Nobody is trapped here.

## Part Four: Roles and Labour

### 4.1 Domain Structure

The community's work divides into eight domains. Every adult member has a primary domain and is expected to contribute to commons work across domains as needed.

**Domain 1: Food Production**
Grains, vegetables, legumes, fruit. The core of material life. Approximately 30–35% of adult labour hours in peak season, less in winter.

**Domain 2: Animal Husbandry**
Dairy, eggs, meat, draught animals, fibre animals. Approximately 15% of adult labour hours year-round (animals require daily attention regardless of season).

**Domain 3: Water and Sanitation**
System maintenance, monitoring, seasonal management. Lower ongoing labour (2–5% of hours) but absolutely critical. Cannot be neglected.

**Domain 4: Building and Maintenance**
Construction, repair, tool maintenance, roads and paths. Approximately 10–15% of adult labour hours, heavier in early years.

**Domain 5: Food Preservation and Processing**
Milling, fermentation, curing, drying, storage management. Approximately 10% of adult labour hours, heavily concentrated in harvest season.

**Domain 6: Health and Medicine**
Care of the sick, childbirth support, herb growing and medicine-making, basic surgery, mental health support. Approximately 5–8% of adult labour hours but absolutely critical. Requires dedicated trained individuals.

**Domain 7: Education and Knowledge**
Children's education, adult skill-sharing, library curation, apprenticeship coordination. Approximately 5% of adult labour hours, plus the informal teaching embedded in all other domains.

**Domain 8: Commons and Culture**
Gathering space maintenance, celebration planning, music, art, storytelling, spiritual life, visitor relations, external communication. Approximately 5% of adult labour hours. Not a luxury — as essential as food.

### 4.2 Labour Accounting

The community does not pay wages. It does not keep individual accounts. But it should track aggregate labour patterns across domains to identify imbalances — not to punish low contributors but to ensure no domain is chronically under-resourced.

Suggested approach: each domain lead estimates quarterly hours needed versus available. Full Assembly reviews quarterly. Rebalancing is a community conversation, not an administrative penalty.

**The free rider problem.** In a genuine community of mutual care at 150 people, the free rider problem is much smaller than theorists assume, because everyone is visible. People who are genuinely not contributing are seen. Community culture handles most cases before they become governance problems. For the cases it does not, Level 1–3 conflict resolution applies. The answer to free riders is almost never punitive accounting — it is conversation, relationship, and occasionally departure.

### 4.3 Skill Inventory and Training

In the first year, conduct a full skills audit of all adult members. Map current capacity against community needs. Identify critical gaps. Create apprenticeship pairings.

**Critical skills to ensure are held by multiple people (minimum 3 per skill):**
- Seed saving and selection
- Grain milling and bread-making
- Fermentation (vegetable, dairy, grain)
- Animal midwifery and basic veterinary care
- Human midwifery
- Basic surgery and wound care
- Herbalism and medicine preparation
- Water system maintenance and repair
- Basic carpentry and joinery
- Masonry and earthen building
- Blacksmithing and tool repair
- Textile production (spinning, weaving, dyeing)
- Child education
- Conflict mediation

No skill should be held by only one person. When a single person holds a critical skill, the community is one accident away from losing it.

## Part Five: Farming Guide

### 5.1 Land Allocation

| Land Use | Acres | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Grain (wheat, rye, oats, corn) | 180–200 | Includes rotation fallow |
| Legumes (beans, lentils, peas) | 80–100 | Also nitrogen-fixing |
| Vegetables and alliums | 30–40 | High labour, high nutrition |
| Fruit and nut trees | 40–50 | Long investment, long return |
| Pasture | 100–120 | Sheep, goats, cattle |
| Fodder and hay | 50–60 | Winter animal feed |
| Woodland garden | 50–60 | Managed polyculture |
| Market garden (trade surplus) | 20–30 | Optional, for external trade |

### 5.2 The Three-Field Rotation System

Divide arable land into three roughly equal blocks and rotate annually:

**Year 1 — Winter grain.** Wheat or rye. Planted in autumn, harvested following summer. Highest calorie yield per acre.

**Year 2 — Spring grain and legumes.** Oats or barley plus beans or peas intercropped or in strips. The legumes fix atmospheric nitrogen, partially restoring what the grain removed.

**Year 3 — Fallow.** The field rests. Graze animals on it to add manure. Plant green manure crops (clover, vetch) and turn them in. This field recovers its fertility for the next cycle.

This system, used across Europe for a thousand years before synthetic fertilizers, is sustainable indefinitely on good soil with adequate animal manure inputs.

### 5.3 Soil Management

**The core principle: feed the soil, not the plant.** A living soil with high organic matter, diverse microbial communities, and earthworm populations will grow healthy plants without external inputs. A depleted soil will not, regardless of what you add to it.

**Compost.** Every organic waste product — kitchen scraps, animal manure, crop residues, wood ash — goes back to the land through compost. Maintain three compost systems in rotation. Hot compost (properly managed) kills pathogens and weed seeds. Target: 2–4 inches of compost applied to planting beds annually.

**Animal integration.** Animals and crops are not separate systems. They are one system. Animal manure is the fertility engine. Move animals across the land in rotation — where animals have grazed and dunged, next year's crops grow better. The two-legged and four-legged economies are the same economy.

**Cover cropping.** Never leave soil bare. Bare soil loses moisture, erodes, and loses microbial communities. When a field is not in production, it is under a cover crop — clover, vetch, buckwheat, winter rye. These are turned in before the next planting.

**No-dig beds.** For the vegetable garden, adopt permanent raised beds that are never walked on and never tilled. Soil structure builds over years. Productivity increases without increasing labour. Sheet mulch with organic matter annually rather than digging.

### 5.4 Grain Production

**Target yields (low-tech, good management):** 700–900 lbs/acre for wheat and rye; 800–1000 lbs/acre for oats.

**Seed selection.** Each year, select seed from the best-performing plants in your fields — largest heads, latest to lodge, earliest to mature, most resistant to disease. Save 10–15% more than you need. Seed selection over 5–10 years dramatically improves your specific-site-adapted varieties. This is among the most important work the community does.

**Harvest.** With scythes and hand tools, a team of 20 fit adults can harvest approximately 5 acres per day. Plan the harvest accordingly. This is community-wide work — everyone who can participate does.

**Milling.** A small watermill or windmill serving 150 people requires a millstone of approximately 3–4 feet diameter running 4–6 hours per day at peak demand. This is achievable with a modest stream or reliable wind. Without mechanical milling, hand milling grain for 150 people consumes several hours daily — the mill is among the most valuable technologies you can build.

**Storage.** Grain must be stored dry, cool, and sealed against rodents. Stone or brick granaries with thick walls, raised floors, and cat patrols have worked for millennia. Target: 18 months of grain in storage at all times, providing buffer against one crop failure without starvation.

### 5.5 Animal Husbandry

**Recommended starting herd for 150 people:**

| Animal | Number | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Dairy cows | 10–12 | Milk, butter, cheese, occasional meat |
| Goats | 15–20 | Milk (especially for children and elderly), cheese, meat, brush clearing |
| Pigs | 8–10 | Waste conversion, meat, lard |
| Chickens | 100–150 | Eggs daily, meat, insect control |
| Sheep | 20–30 | Wool, milk, meat, land management |
| Draught animals | 4–6 oxen or horses | Field work, hauling |
| Bees | 10–15 hives | Pollination, honey, wax |

**The daily reality of animals.** Animals require care every single day of the year, including holidays and illness and bad weather. This is the most scheduling-constrained domain in the community. Roster the animal care team with explicit backup coverage for every role. Never assume informal coverage — someone's cow gets milked twice a day whether it is planned or not.

**Breeding strategy.** Maintain sufficient breeding stock to be fully self-sufficient in animal reproduction. Do not become dependent on purchasing animals from outside. Learn basic animal midwifery and keep a veterinary herb garden. Build a relationship with a sympathetic external veterinarian for genuine emergencies but do not structure your system around their availability.

### 5.6 The Vegetable Garden

Vegetables are labour-intensive but nutritionally critical and highly productive per square foot. Target 200–300 square feet of intensive garden bed per person.

**Year-round production calendar (temperate climate):**

*Winter (beds under cover where needed):* Kale, leeks, root vegetables in storage, winter salad greens under cold frames.

*Early Spring:* Broad beans, peas, lettuce, spinach, overwintered onions.

*Late Spring:* Transplant tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers, squash. Direct sow carrots, beetroot, more beans.

*Summer:* Peak production. Harvest continuously. Begin preservation immediately — do not allow surplus to rot.

*Autumn:* Root vegetable harvest and storage. Brassicas. Last tomatoes. Begin winter preparations.

**Preservation must keep pace with production.** A garden that produces more than can be preserved is not a functioning system. Preservation capacity (fermentation crocks, drying racks, root cellars, salt supply) must be planned before planting, not after harvest.

### 5.7 Orchard and Woodland

Fruit and nut trees are the community's longest investment and its most stable food source. A well-established orchard requires relatively little annual labour while producing abundantly. Plant the orchard in the first year.

**Recommended mix (temperate climate):**
- Apples and pears (cider and eating varieties — store well, press for winter drink)
- Plums and damsons (jam and drying)
- Chestnuts (high-calorie nut, stores well, partly substitutes grain)
- Hazelnuts (high-calorie nut, coppice for other uses)
- Walnuts (oil, eating, preserving)
- Mulberries (abundant, low-maintenance, medicine)
- Elderberry and elderflower (medicine, drink, food)
- Quince (preserves, medicine)

**Forest garden.** Beyond the formal orchard, establish a managed woodland garden with multiple layers: canopy trees, understory fruit and nut trees, shrubs (berry, medicinal), ground layer (herbs, vegetables), climbers (hop, grape), and root layer (Jerusalem artichoke, comfrey). This system, once established, produces food with minimal intervention and builds soil over time.

## Part Six: Infrastructure

### 6.1 Water Systems

**The gravity-fed system is your goal.** Find your spring or stream intake point above the settlement. Lay pipes (clay tile historically; recycled plastic acceptable if available) from intake to a settling tank, then to a distribution point at the settlement. From there, gravity delivers water to key points without pumping.

**Key water points to serve:**
- Central village fountain (social hub, not just utility)
- Kitchen and food processing buildings
- Animal watering points
- Laundry and bathing facilities
- Garden irrigation (gravity drip or channel)
- Fire protection cistern

**Water quality.** Test your source. Know what you have. Simple sand and charcoal filtration removes most contaminants. Boiling remains the most reliable pathogen control. If you are uncertain about your source, build a slow-sand filter before distributing to drinking points.

**Sanitation.** Separate grey water (washing) from black water (human waste) from the beginning. Grey water can irrigate non-food plants after simple settling. Black water requires composting toilets or a properly designed and sited pit system, always downhill and downwind of water sources and dwellings. Germ theory tells you everything you need to know: get human waste away from water and food. The specific technology matters less than the principle.

### 6.2 Buildings

**Design for climate.** The single most important building design principle is passive climate response. A well-designed building in a temperate climate maintains comfortable temperatures with minimal active heating or cooling.

**Passive solar design basics:**
- Orient main living spaces to face south (northern hemisphere) or north (southern hemisphere)
- Large windows on the sun-facing side; small windows on the shade side
- Thermal mass (stone, brick, earth) inside the sun-facing glass to absorb heat by day and release it by night
- Deep roof overhangs to shade the glass in summer (when the sun is high) but allow winter sun (low) to enter
- Good insulation in roof and walls
- Earth sheltering on the north side where possible

**Building materials.** Use what is local and abundant. Stone, timber, earth (cob, adobe, rammed earth), lime plaster. These materials are repairable, recyclable, and do not require global supply chains. Avoid materials whose production or transport requires the industrial system — even if they are currently cheap and available, plan for the day they are not.

**The commons buildings** should be the best-built structures in the community: the meeting hall, the kitchen, the school, the medical room, the library. Private dwellings can be simple. Shared spaces should be beautiful.

### 6.3 Energy

**Primary heat: wood.** Managed coppice woodland provides sustainable wood fuel indefinitely. Coppicing (cutting trees to the stump and allowing regrowth) produces poles and fuel on 7–20 year rotation depending on species. Hazel, ash, willow, and sweet chestnut are classic coppice species. With well-designed rocket mass heaters or masonry stoves, wood consumption per household is a fraction of conventional wood burning.

**Light.** Beeswax candles and rendered tallow from animal fat provide light. Oil lamps burning vegetable oil (pressed from sunflower, linseed, or similar). If solar panels are available and pass the technology filter assessment, battery-backed LED lighting is a significant quality-of-life improvement at very low maintenance cost — once installed, solar panels last 25–30 years without attention.

**Mechanical power.** Mill your grain with water or wind. Use animal draught for heavy field work. Bicycle-powered tools for small-scale mechanical tasks. These replace fossil fuel energy for the community's primary needs.

## Part Seven: Food Preservation

This is among the most critical domains. Without preservation, abundance becomes scarcity seasonally. With it, the community eats well year-round from its summer peak production.

### 7.1 Fermentation

Fermentation is the deepest food technology humanity possesses. It preserves, transforms, and often improves the nutritional value of food simultaneously. Every community member should understand it. The domain specialists should master it completely.

**Lacto-fermentation of vegetables.** Sauerkraut, kimchi, fermented beets, pickled cucumbers, fermented beans. Salt draws water from vegetables, creating brine in which lactobacillus bacteria (present on all vegetables) preserve the food for months. No refrigeration needed. Equipment required: crocks, weights, salt. This is the preservation backbone for the vegetable garden.

**Dairy fermentation.** Yoghurt, kefir, butter, and cheese transform fresh milk into products that last days (yoghurt), weeks (hard cheese), or years (aged cheese). Cheese-making requires rennet (from animal stomachs or plant sources) and controlled temperature environments. A cool stone cheese cave is a significant community asset.

**Grain fermentation.** Beer, ale, and cider are not luxuries — they are preserved calories, safer than water before germ theory was applied, and sources of B vitamins. More importantly: they are made from abundant seasonal surpluses (grain, apples) and store for months. A community brewery is a serious food security asset, not a social amenity.

**Sourdough.** A living sourdough culture makes leavened bread from grain and water alone, with no commercial yeast. One maintained culture, fed regularly, serves a community indefinitely. It also partially predigests grain, improving nutrient availability.

### 7.2 Root Cellaring

A well-constructed root cellar — ideally earth-sheltered, oriented north, with stone or brick walls and a packed earth floor — maintains temperatures of 32–40°F (0–4°C) with moderate humidity year-round without any active refrigeration.

In these conditions, the following store for months: carrots, beets, turnips, parsnips, potatoes, cabbage, apples, pears, celeriac, winter squash, onions, garlic, dried beans, dried grains.

Size the root cellar generously. You will always wish you had built it larger.

### 7.3 Drying and Salting

**Drying** removes water from food, preventing microbial growth. Herbs, mushrooms, beans, peas, sliced fruit, and some vegetables dry in air and sun. A solar drier (a simple glass-covered box) accelerates and protects the process. Dried goods store for 1–3 years in cool, dry conditions.

**Salt curing** preserves meat and fish for months to years. Requires a reliable salt supply — consider this in site selection and trading relationships. Smoked meat combines salt, drying, and antimicrobial smoke compounds. A community smokehouse is a significant long-term asset.

## Part Eight: Health and Medicine

### 8.1 Prevention First

The single highest-return health investment is the application of germ theory to daily life. Clean water. Proper sanitation sited away from water and food. Hand washing before food handling and after sanitation use. Clean birth practices. Wound cleaning.

These cost essentially nothing. They save more lives than any other health intervention.

**The medical garden.** Maintain a dedicated garden of medicinal plants. Many common medicines were derived from plants and their plant precursors remain effective. Essential inclusions:

*Wound and infection:* Calendula, yarrow, plantain, garlic, honey (antibacterial), lavender.

*Fever and inflammation:* Willow bark (salicylic acid — aspirin's ancestor), elderflower, peppermint.

*Respiratory:* Thyme, horehound, elderberry, mullein, elecampane.

*Digestive:* Chamomile, ginger, fennel, meadowsweet, slippery elm.

*Nervous system:* Valerian, passionflower, lemon balm, St. John's Wort.

*Women's health:* Raspberry leaf, motherwort, blue cohosh (birth), shepherd's purse (postpartum bleeding).

### 8.2 Skilled Practitioners

The community must have — not aspire to have, but actually have — the following trained practitioners before reaching full population:

**Midwife.** Competent in normal birth, common complications, and postpartum care. Trained by an experienced practitioner, not books alone. This is the most critical medical role. Childbirth complications that are handled routinely in hospitals are life-threatening without competent assistance.

**Wound care and basic surgery.** Knowledge of cleaning, suturing, and dressing wounds. Appendectomy technique (an appendectomy has been performed successfully in a submarine — the skill is achievable). Bone-setting. Recognition of conditions requiring external hospital referral.

**Herbalist.** Deep knowledge of the medical garden and local wild medicinals. Preparation of tinctures, salves, teas, and poultices. Understanding of contraindications and dosages. Not mysticism — applied botanical pharmacology.

**Mental health support.** A community of 150 people will contain people experiencing depression, anxiety, grief, trauma, and relational difficulty. Someone needs to be trained and trusted to hold space for this. This role is often undervalued until it is desperately needed.

### 8.3 The External Medicine Line

Know what you can handle internally and what you cannot. Agree in advance on the conditions that trigger immediate external medical intervention. Maintain this relationship with the outside medical system without shame or ideological resistance. The goal is not self-sufficiency in surgery — it is minimising unnecessary dependence while maintaining honest capacity assessment.

Keep a supply of core medicines that you cannot produce internally: antibiotics (discuss with a sympathetic doctor), epinephrine (for anaphylaxis), insulin (if any community member requires it), appropriate prescription medications for chronic conditions. These are not compromise — they are prudence.

## Part Nine: Education and Knowledge

### 9.1 The School

Children are not a category separate from community life — they are the community's future and its most important current members. Education should be integrated into daily work where possible, not segregated from it.

**The goal of Eden education:**
- Full literacy and numeracy
- Deep ecological knowledge of the local land (plants, animals, seasons, weather)
- Practical skills across all community domains, introduced progressively by age
- History and philosophy — why we are here, what was lost, what was chosen
- Music, drawing, storytelling — the full human range
- Critical thinking and healthy skepticism
- Knowledge of the outside world — not to romanticise it, but to understand it

**The teaching approach.** Formal teaching for younger children (ages 5–10): 3–4 hours daily, mornings. Afternoons: apprenticeship and participation in domain work appropriate to age. Older children (10–15): increasing domain work alongside continued formal teaching. No child should feel the school is separate from the life the community lives.

**Who teaches.** Everyone teaches something. Formal coordination of younger children's education requires dedicated people (suggest: 2–3 adults per 20 children). But the community's collective knowledge is the curriculum.

### 9.2 The Library

Print matters. Maintain a physical library of reference works that can function without any electronic infrastructure.

**Essential holdings:**

*Agriculture:* The One-Straw Revolution (Fukuoka). Farmers of Forty Centuries (King). Gaia's Garden (Hemenway). The Market Gardener (Fortier). Seed to Seed (Ashworth). Any comprehensive agricultural almanac relevant to your climate.

*Medicine:* Where There Is No Doctor (Werner). Where There Is No Dentist (Dickson). A Complete Herbal (Culpeper, plus modern verification). Merck Manual (most recent available edition).

*Building:* The Hand-Sculpted House (Evans). Building with Earth (Norton). Timber Framing for the Rest of Us (Roy).

*Food preservation:* The Art of Fermentation (Katz). Root Cellaring (Bubel). Preserving Food Without Freezing or Canning (Terre Vivante).

*Governance and community:* Creating a Life Together (Christian). A Pattern Language (Alexander). Elinor Ostrom's work on commons governance.

*Philosophy and spirit:* Whatever speaks to the community's core. The texts that remind you why you chose this. Maintain these as carefully as the farming manuals.

Protect the library from damp, fire, and rodents. Copy deteriorating volumes by hand if necessary. The knowledge that does not need to be rediscovered from scratch is among the greatest gifts one generation gives the next.

## Part Ten: Trade and External Relations

### 10.1 The Principle of Selective Engagement

The community is not an island. Attempting to be one fails. The goal is sovereignty, not isolation — the ability to engage the outside world on your own terms rather than its terms.

Engage externally for: things you genuinely cannot produce (salt, metal stock, medicine, specific seeds, books), occasional skilled services (specialist medical, legal), and the maintenance of relationships that keep you human and connected.

Do not engage externally for: things that create ongoing dependency, things that require you to produce beyond your needs in ways that distort community life, things that are simply cheaper outside but available inside.

### 10.2 What to Trade

Your surplus is your trading currency. Surpluses the community might reliably generate: high-quality preserved foods, artisan goods (cheese, fermented products, textiles, pottery, woodwork), fresh seasonal produce, seeds of locally-adapted varieties, skilled labour exchanges with neighbouring communities.

Do not trade away seed stock, breeding animals, core tools, or anything whose loss would compromise community security.

### 10.3 Neighbouring Communities

The best long-term security is a network of similarly-minded communities within trading and mutual-aid distance. You can specialise slightly — one community focuses on cheese, another on metalwork, another on medicine — without any community becoming dependent on the others.

Formalise mutual aid agreements: in the event of crop failure, fire, illness, or other crisis, neighbouring communities provide stated support. You extend the same. This is the ancient system of community insurance that functioned for millennia before financial instruments replaced it.

## Part Eleven: The Spiritual Dimension

### 11.1 The Church on the Hill

Whatever form it takes — chapel, meditation hall, stone circle, simple clearing with a view — the community needs a gathering point that is explicitly not about material life. A space where the question "what is this all for?" is not merely permitted but required.

This is not optional. Communities that neglect the meaning-making dimension of human life do not fail through crop failure. They fail through emptiness — through the slow leakage of people toward a world that offers sensation if not meaning.

The church on the hill serves three functions:

**Vertical orientation.** It acknowledges that the community is embedded in something larger than itself — whether that is understood as God, nature, the cosmos, the ancestors, or simply the long arc of human story. This acknowledgment is not weakness. It is accuracy.

**Weekly gathering.** Once a week, everyone comes to the same place for the same reason. Grudges are formally set aside. Deaths are mourned. Births are celebrated. The year's rhythms are marked. This gathering is the operating system update — the moment the community re-syncs its shared understanding of itself.

**Beauty.** The community's most beautiful object, placed in the most visible location. The act of building something beautiful that serves no material purpose is itself the point. It says: we do not live by bread alone. We knew exactly what bread cost. We built this anyway.

### 11.2 Celebration and Rhythm

Mark the year. Eight festivals minimum, aligned with the agricultural and astronomical calendar: solstices, equinoxes, and the four cross-quarter days that mark the midpoints of the seasons. These are not decorative — they are the community's shared temporal framework, its sense of location in time as the fields provide its location in space.

Music. Story. Dance. These are not amenities. They are the technology by which communities encode values, process collective experience, and pass identity to the next generation. Maintain them with the same seriousness as the farming calendar.

## Part Twelve: Bootstrap Sequence

### Year Zero — Before You Arrive

- Conduct full skills audit of founding members. Identify critical gaps. Fill them.
- Establish the Core Agreements. Read them. Sign them. Mean them.
- Secure land with clear title.
- Commission soil tests and water quality analysis.
- Design site layout on paper before touching ground.
- Establish the root cellar and grain storage structures as first priority.
- Assemble: hand tools, seed stock (open-pollinated/heritage varieties), basic medical supplies, core library, fermentation equipment, basic carpentry tools.
- Identify external allies: a doctor, a veterinarian, a lawyer, an engineer sympathetic to the project.

### Year One — Foundations

- Build temporary shelters first. Permanent dwellings second.
- Plant the orchard immediately — it is the longest investment.
- Establish the water system before anything else.
- Plant a market garden for immediate food security while grain infrastructure is built.
- Establish composting systems.
- Begin the milking herd — animal products provide caloric buffer while grain production scales.
- Hold weekly assemblies. Resolve disputes early. Do not let grievances accumulate.
- Document everything. Crop yields, animal health, water quality, labour hours. You will need this data.

### Year Two — Stabilisation

- Full grain production underway.
- Begin building permanent structures systematically.
- Establish the three-field rotation.
- First preservation season — everything from summer stored for winter.
- Begin apprenticeship pairings for critical skills.
- Establish trade relationships with neighbouring communities or towns.
- Review the Core Agreements. Amend as reality requires. Do not be precious about the founding documents.

### Year Three — Maturity

- Grain storage target: 18 months of supply.
- Animal herd at target population.
- Medical practitioner fully trained and practising.
- School formally operating.
- Library established.
- The community has survived at least one hard season and knows how it responds to difficulty.
- Begin considering carefully managed membership expansion.

### Year Five — Assessment

At five years, conduct a full honest assessment:

- Is the land improving or degrading?
- Are people leaving? Why?
- Are children thriving?
- Is the daily labour burden sustainable long-term?
- Are the Core Agreements still alive or have they become formality?
- What have you learned that the playbook got wrong?

Then rewrite the relevant sections of this document with what you know.

## Appendix A: Essential Tool List

**Field tools (per 5 adults):** 5 scythes with blades and snaths, 5 long-handled hoes, 5 spades, 5 forks, 2 broadforks, 2 wheel hoes, 1 ox-drawn plough or equivalent, harvesting baskets, grain storage sacks.

**Medical:** Surgical kit (scalpel, sutures, forceps, retractors), sterilisation equipment, blood pressure cuff, stethoscope, thermometers, bandaging stock, splinting materials.

**Communications:** Short-wave radio receiver. At least one bicycle per adult. Reliable horse or equivalent for emergency distance travel.

## Appendix B: The Single Most Important Thing

Everything in this document is secondary to one thing.

**The people.**

Land can be found. Skills can be learned. Buildings can be built. Tools can be made or traded for.

What cannot be improvised is a group of 150 people who genuinely mean it — who have chosen this consciously, who understand what they are refusing, who are capable of the sustained, ordinary, daily work of caring for each other and the land without romance and without resentment.

Finding those people is the work that precedes all other work.

When you have them, the rest follows.

When you do not have them, nothing else matters.
