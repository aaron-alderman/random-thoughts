# The Judgment Preservation Project
## Diagnostic Framework: Recognising Judgment Erosion
### Signs, Indicators, and Early Warnings at the Individual, Institutional, and Civilisational Levels

---

> *"The first sign of decay is usually invisible from the inside."*

---

## A Note on the Nature of These Metrics

Judgment erosion is not like a broken bone. It does not announce itself with sudden pain. It is more like the slow loss of muscle through disuse — gradual, quiet, and often not noticed until the moment of need reveals the absence of what was once there.

This creates a fundamental diagnostic challenge: the very capacity needed to detect erosion is itself subject to erosion. A person who has significantly lost independent judgment may feel entirely confident in their judgments, because they have no clear memory of what more rigorous independent thinking felt like. An institution whose human competence has been hollowed out by automation may appear to be functioning normally right up until the moment it catastrophically fails.

This framework therefore aims to provide external and behavioural indicators — observable signs that do not rely solely on the subject's own self-assessment. It works at three levels: **individual**, **institutional**, and **civilisational**, and for each level distinguishes between:

- **Early warnings** — subtle signs that erosion has begun
- **Active indicators** — clear evidence that erosion is underway
- **Critical markers** — signs that erosion has become severe and self-reinforcing

A final section offers a structured **self-assessment tool** for individuals and institutions.

---

## Part One: Individual Level

### What We Are Looking For

At the individual level, judgment erosion shows up in the cognitive habits, emotional responses, relational patterns, and practical capabilities of a person. The key question is not "how much does this person use AI?" but "has their capacity for independent thought, evaluation, and responsible decision-making diminished?"

Heavy AI use is not itself a sign of erosion. The person who uses AI extensively but maintains vigorous independent reasoning, regularly challenges AI outputs, and demonstrates growing domain competence over time is not eroding. The person who uses AI moderately but has quietly stopped forming their own views, struggles to evaluate claims without AI assistance, and feels anxious when AI is unavailable — that person may be well into the erosion process.

---

### Early Warnings: Individual

**1. The Blank Page Discomfort**

The person finds it noticeably more difficult to begin tasks — writing, analysis, planning, problem-solving — without first consulting AI for orientation or a starting point. Where previously they would have simply begun, there is now a new pause, a pull toward the prompt box before pen meets paper or thought meets thought.

*What to watch for:* Routinely opening an AI interface before attempting independent engagement with a task. Feeling blocked or anxious when asked to produce something without AI access. Using AI to "get started" on tasks that previously required no such scaffold.

*The distinction that matters:* Using AI to assist after forming an initial view is healthy. Using AI because forming an initial view has become uncomfortable is an early warning sign.

---

**2. Reduced Tolerance for Ambiguity**

The person becomes less comfortable sitting with unresolved questions, competing considerations, or genuine uncertainty. There is an increasing preference for clear answers — even when the situation doesn't warrant them — and a growing impatience with the kind of sustained, inconclusive thinking that genuine complexity requires.

AI systems provide answers. They rarely say "I genuinely don't know, and you should sit with this for a while." Regular AI consultation may subtly train users to expect resolution that is not always appropriate.

*What to watch for:* Rushing to conclusions on complex matters. Discomfort in discussions that don't arrive at answers. Reduced ability to hold a question open across multiple days or sessions. Preference for AI-generated certainty over humanly honest uncertainty.

---

**3. Weakening Verification Instinct**

The automatic impulse to check, question, or verify information from any source — including AI — begins to fade. The person starts accepting AI outputs with less scrutiny than they would have previously applied to other authoritative-sounding sources.

*What to watch for:* Citing AI-generated information without having checked it. Treating AI outputs as ground truth rather than as starting points. Reduced use of primary sources, original documents, or authoritative references. Difficulty recalling the last time they actually verified an AI claim.

---

**4. Formative Practices Quietly Dropped**

The person has stopped doing things they used to do — reading deeply, writing reflectively, working through problems on paper, engaging in sustained debate — without consciously deciding to stop. The practices have simply faded because AI provides a faster route to similar-seeming outputs.

*What to watch for:* Books started but not finished because "I can just ask AI." Journals or reflective writing abandoned. Mental arithmetic, map-reading, or other practical cognitive skills no longer practiced. The realization, when asked, that certain intellectual habits were last exercised months or years ago.

---

### Active Indicators: Individual

**5. Framing Capture**

The person routinely accepts the AI's framing of a problem rather than defining the problem themselves. Their thinking about an issue is shaped by how the AI presented it, and they would find it difficult to articulate the same issue in substantially different terms.

*What to watch for:* Using the AI's structure, categories, and vocabulary when discussing topics they have researched using AI. Difficulty reframing a problem when asked. Noticing, when challenged, that their "view" on something largely matches the AI's response.

*The test:* Ask someone to explain their view on a topic they recently researched using AI — and then ask them to explain it from a fundamentally different angle. If reframing is difficult, framing capture may have occurred.

---

**6. Discomfort with Disagreement**

The person finds it increasingly uncomfortable to maintain a position against confident opposition — including AI outputs that contradict their prior view. There is a growing pull toward updating toward whatever the AI says, not because of genuine persuasion but because of the social and cognitive weight of confident-sounding authoritative output.

*What to watch for:* Frequently updating views immediately after AI pushback without being able to articulate why the update is warranted. Difficulty defending a position against AI-generated counterarguments even when those counterarguments are weak. A pattern of eventually agreeing with AI outputs regardless of initial disagreement.

---

**7. Domain Hollowing**

In areas of claimed expertise or significant prior knowledge, the person's actual independent capability has quietly diminished. They know more about a domain in the sense of having access to more information through AI, but know less in the sense of being able to reason, evaluate, or operate in that domain without AI assistance.

*What to watch for:* Struggling to explain the reasoning behind professional judgments they routinely make. Being unable to complete domain tasks to previous standards when AI is unavailable. Feeling less confident in professional settings where AI cannot be consulted. The sense that expertise has become navigation of AI tools rather than genuine mastery.

---

**8. Relational Substitution**

The person increasingly turns to AI for what were previously relational or communal needs: emotional processing, moral reasoning, spiritual direction, personal advice. This is not just about getting information — it is about seeking the kind of engagement that should come from trusted human relationships.

*What to watch for:* Regularly processing emotional difficulties with AI rather than trusted people. Seeking moral guidance from AI rather than community, tradition, or pastoral relationships. Feeling better understood by AI than by the people in one's life — and accepting rather than questioning this feeling. Reduced investment in the human relationships that previously provided counsel.

---

### Critical Markers: Individual

**9. The Inability to Operate Independently**

The person genuinely cannot complete significant professional or intellectual tasks to an adequate standard without AI assistance. This is not preference or efficiency — it is actual incapacity. The skill is gone.

*What to watch for:* Open admission of inability to perform tasks without AI. Significant anxiety or paralysis when AI is unavailable. Marked decline in output quality on AI-unavailable assessments. The person can describe what good work looks like but cannot produce it independently.

---

**10. Loss of Epistemic Ownership**

The person can no longer clearly distinguish between views they have genuinely formed and views they have absorbed from AI. When asked "what do you think?" they produce something that is essentially an AI summary of common positions on the topic, with no clear indication of personal evaluation, experience, or commitment.

*What to watch for:* Responses to opinion questions that are balanced to the point of having no actual position. Inability to identify which of their views they hold most confidently and why. The sense that "what I think" has become "what AI told me about what people think."

---

**11. Anxiety as a Diagnostic**

Perhaps the most telling individual marker is the quality of the anxiety produced by AI unavailability. A person who uses AI heavily but retains independent judgment will find AI unavailability inconvenient. A person who has experienced significant erosion will find it genuinely distressing — not because of the lost efficiency, but because of the felt loss of cognitive capacity. The anxiety reveals a dependency.

---

- 10–20: Healthy engagement. Some vigilance warranted.
- 21–30: Early erosion possible. Review which specific areas score highest.
- 31–40: Active erosion likely. Immediate practice review recommended.
- 41–50: Significant erosion. Substantial intervention needed.

## Part Two: Institutional Level

### What We Are Looking For

At the institutional level — churches, schools, hospitals, law firms, government agencies, businesses — judgment erosion shows up in the practices, standards, culture, and structural arrangements that either maintain or undermine human competence. The key questions are: does this institution genuinely require and develop human judgment? Does it maintain the standards, training, and culture that make real human competence a live force? Or has it, quietly and incrementally, substituted AI-mediated process for genuine human thinking?

Institutions are particularly vulnerable to a specific failure mode: they can appear to be functioning normally while their actual human judgment capacity has been substantially hollowed. Performance metrics are maintained because AI maintains them. Standards appear to be met because AI meets them. The hollowing only becomes visible when the AI is wrong, unavailable, or exploited — at which point there is no independent human competence to catch the failure.

---

### Early Warnings: Institutional

**12. Assessment and Evaluation Drift**

The institution's formal assessment and evaluation processes have quietly shifted to reward AI-assisted outputs indistinguishable from human ones. Nobody made a decision to accept AI-generated work — the standards simply drifted, gradually, to accommodate what was being submitted.

*What to watch for in education:* Assignment formats that can be entirely completed by AI without meaningful penalty. Teachers who cannot identify AI-generated work in their subject. A gap between formal policies (AI not permitted) and actual practice (AI routinely used and accepted). Assessment criteria that reward plausible-sounding outputs rather than demonstrated reasoning.

*What to watch for in professional settings:* Performance reviews that evaluate outputs without examining the process by which they were produced. Advancement criteria that can be met through AI-mediated performance. Declining ability of managers to assess actual competence as opposed to AI-assisted output quality.

---

**13. Training Programme Compression**

The institution has shortened, simplified, or restructured training and development programmes on the grounds that AI tools reduce the time needed to develop competence. The assumption is that AI fills the gap. The reality is that the training was developing judgment, not just transmitting information — and AI cannot fill that gap.

*What to watch for:* Medical residency or apprenticeship programmes shortened because AI handles the tasks they were designed to develop. Professional training focused on tool use rather than domain reasoning. Mentorship relationships replaced by AI onboarding. The assumption that a junior practitioner equipped with AI tools is equivalent to a senior practitioner without them.

---

**14. Institutional Memory Shifting to AI**

Knowledge that was previously held in the heads and practice of experienced human members of the institution is shifting to AI systems. People increasingly ask AI rather than asking colleagues. The informal networks of expertise — the "ask Dave, he knows about this" culture — are quietly atrophying.

*What to watch for:* Declining consultation between experienced and newer staff. The perception that AI provides faster and more reliable answers than colleagues. Retirement of experienced practitioners without meaningful knowledge transfer processes. The institution's accumulated practical wisdom residing increasingly in AI prompting habits rather than human minds.

---

**15. Risk Aversion Paradox**

The institution uses AI partly to reduce the risk of human error — but this creates a new risk profile: correlated failures rather than independent ones. When the AI is wrong, everyone using it is wrong in the same way simultaneously. The institution's leadership does not appear to have recognised this shift in risk architecture.

*What to watch for:* Uniform AI-mediated responses across an institution to situations that previously would have received diverse human responses. Absence of adversarial review processes for AI-generated decisions. Risk frameworks that account for individual human error but not for systematic AI error. Leaders who can articulate the risks of human judgment but not the distinctive risks of AI-mediated judgment.

---

### Active Indicators: Institutional

**16. The Competence Gap Becomes Visible**

There is a growing, measurable gap between what the institution's members can do with AI and what they can do without it. This gap has become apparent in specific situations — IT failures, edge cases, crisis responses — where AI-unassisted performance was required and fell short.

*What to watch for:* Acknowledged instances of poor performance when AI tools were unavailable. Situations in which practitioners were unable to exercise independent professional judgment in areas that should be core competencies. Informal conversation about how "we used to know how to do this." Senior leadership aware but uncertain how to address the gap.

---

**17. Standards Explicitly Revised Downward**

The institution has formally revised professional standards, curricula, or competency frameworks in ways that reduce requirements for independent human judgment — often framed as "updating for the AI era" or "redefining what competence means." The revision is presented as progress, not retreat.

*What to watch for:* Accreditation or licensing frameworks that now accept AI-assisted performance where independent performance was previously required. Professional bodies that have removed or significantly reduced requirements for demonstrating unaided competence. Educational curricula that treat AI-tool proficiency as a substitute for domain knowledge.

---

**18. Accountability Structures Eroded**

The institution's mechanisms for holding human practitioners accountable for their judgments have weakened, partly because AI mediation has made it harder to establish clear human responsibility. When AI is involved in a decision that turns out to be wrong, accountability is diffuse or absent.

*What to watch for:* Difficulty, in post-incident reviews, identifying who made which decision and on what basis. Practitioners who describe following AI recommendations as the extent of their responsibility. Legal or disciplinary frameworks that have not kept pace with the attribution challenges of AI-assisted decision-making. A culture in which deferring to AI is treated as due diligence rather than as a transfer of responsibility.

---

**19. Selection and Hiring Shifts**

The institution's hiring criteria have shifted toward AI tool proficiency and away from domain judgment. Over time, this changes the composition of the institution's membership — toward those who are good at working with AI, and away from those who have developed strong independent judgment.

*What to watch for:* Job descriptions emphasising AI tool proficiency with diminished attention to domain expertise. Hiring panels that can assess AI-assisted outputs but struggle to probe for independent reasoning capacity. A cohort of newer practitioners noticeably more AI-dependent than the cohort they are replacing. Senior practitioners concerned about the judgment depth of their successors.

---

### Critical Markers: Institutional

**20. Crisis Response Failure**

The institution encounters a novel situation — one that the AI systems it relies upon were not designed for or have no reliable training on — and is unable to respond adequately because the human competence that would previously have handled it has been sufficiently eroded. The crisis reveals the hollowing that was invisible during normal operations.

*What to watch for:* This is a lagging indicator — it only appears after erosion is advanced. The appropriate response is to treat it as confirmation of a problem that should have been caught earlier, and to begin aggressive remediation.

---

**21. Knowledge Transmission Failure**

The institution can no longer reliably transmit core domain knowledge to the next generation of practitioners. The knowledge exists in AI systems and in the heads of aging senior practitioners, but the pipeline for developing genuine understanding in the next generation has been broken.

*What to watch for:* Junior practitioners who can produce adequate outputs but cannot explain their reasoning. Senior practitioners who report that they can no longer teach what they know because it has become tacit knowledge that the current training structures do not develop. Acknowledgment that if current senior staff retired tomorrow, the institution's genuine competence would be dramatically reduced.

---

### Institutional Self-Assessment

Rate each item from 1 (not at all) to 5 (clearly true):

| # | Statement | Score |
|---|-----------|-------|
| 1 | Our assessment processes reliably distinguish AI-assisted from independent human performance | |
| 2 | Our training programmes develop genuine domain judgment, not just tool proficiency | |
| 3 | Experienced members' knowledge is actively transferred to newer members through human interaction | |
| 4 | We have clear accountability structures for decisions made with AI assistance | |
| 5 | Our hiring criteria prioritise demonstrated independent judgment | |
| 6 | We regularly test whether our members can perform core tasks without AI assistance | |
| 7 | Our leadership understands the distinctive risk profile of AI-mediated decisions | |
| 8 | We have explicit policies about what decisions require human judgment regardless of AI availability | |
| 9 | We have maintained or increased the depth of training in our core domain over the past three years | |
| 10 | We could continue to function at adequate quality if our primary AI tools were unavailable for one month | |

**Scoring** (note: this is an inverse scale — higher scores indicate health):
- 40–50: Healthy. Maintain current practices and remain vigilant.
- 30–39: Some vulnerability. Identify lowest-scoring areas for targeted attention.
- 20–29: Significant vulnerability. Structured review and remediation needed.
- 10–19: Critical. Institutional judgment capacity may be severely compromised.

## Part Three: Civilisational Level

### What We Are Looking For

At the civilisational level, judgment erosion shows up in the health of the epistemic, deliberative, and institutional systems that allow societies to determine truth, resolve disputes, make collective decisions, and transmit knowledge across generations. These are slower-moving and harder to measure than individual or institutional indicators, but in many ways more consequential.

The civilisational level is also the most difficult to self-assess, because the very capacities needed for assessment — independent critical thinking, access to diverse information sources, institutional trust — are themselves subject to the erosion being measured. This creates the paradox that the societies most severely affected may be among the least able to recognise it.

---

### Early Warnings: Civilisational

**22. Epistemic Monoculture Developing**

Where diverse, independent sources of information and analysis previously existed, a convergent AI-mediated layer is emerging that shapes what most people know and think. The diversity of perspectives that previously provided epistemic resilience is narrowing, not because of censorship but because people are increasingly getting their information from similar AI systems trained on similar data with similar value assumptions.

*What to watch for:* Growing homogeneity of perspective across previously diverse information sources. Declining readership of primary sources as AI summaries substitute for them. Reduced investment in original journalism, research, and analysis as AI synthesis becomes the default. The emergence of "consensus" views that turn out, on examination, to reflect AI defaults rather than genuine intellectual convergence.

---

**23. Verification Infrastructure Weakening**

The social and institutional infrastructure for verifying claims — investigative journalism, academic peer review, independent audit, legal discovery, scientific replication — is under pressure partly because AI-generated content is overwhelming the capacity for human verification. The signal-to-noise ratio in the information environment is worsening faster than verification capacity is developing.

*What to watch for:* Declining resources for investigative journalism. Academic publication systems struggling to identify AI-generated research. Legal and regulatory systems unable to assess AI-generated evidence. A growing population of widely-believed claims that have not been verified by any independent human process.

---

**24. Democratic Deliberation Quality Declining**

The quality of public political deliberation — the ability of citizens to engage seriously with complex policy questions, evaluate competing claims, and form genuine collective judgments — is declining. This is distinct from political polarisation (which may increase even as deliberative quality declines) and from declining trust in institutions (a symptom as much as a cause).

*What to watch for:* Political discourse dominated by AI-generated content and talking points. Declining ability of citizens to identify the key empirical and value questions in complex policy debates. Growing gap between political positions people hold and their ability to defend or explain those positions. Political systems increasingly able to mobilise voters without engaging their genuine judgment.

---

### Active Indicators: Civilisational

**25. Concentration of Epistemic Authority**

The number of entities with genuine capacity to determine what is true on important questions is declining. This concentration takes several forms: fewer media organisations with genuine investigative capacity, fewer independent research institutions, fewer professional bodies capable of maintaining genuine standards, and growing dependence on a small number of AI systems as primary knowledge mediators.

*What to watch for:* Mergers and closures reducing the diversity of serious information sources. Professional bodies unable to maintain genuine standards against AI-mediated performance. A small number of AI platforms effectively setting the terms of public knowledge on major questions. The emergence of a recognisable "AI consensus" on contested questions that treats as settled what remains genuinely debated.

---

**26. Skill Extinction Events**

Entire domains of practical human competence are approaching extinction — not declining gradually but undergoing rapid collapse as the economic basis for maintaining the skill disappears. Once a skill reaches a threshold of rarity, the infrastructure for transmitting it collapses: there are too few practitioners to teach, too few students to learn, and too little community practice to maintain the tacit knowledge that formal training cannot fully capture.

*What to watch for:* Professional communities reporting that they can no longer reliably produce qualified practitioners through existing training pathways. Skills held by an aging cohort with no clear succession. The permanent loss of techniques, practices, or capabilities that were previously considered reliable components of civilisational knowledge.

---

**27. Institutional Legitimacy Crisis**

Democratic and professional institutions are losing legitimacy not primarily because of misconduct or incompetence — though these may be present — but because the genuine human judgment and accountability that gave them their authority is being quietly substituted by AI-mediated process. Institutions that appear to function the same continue to lose authority, and neither they nor the public can clearly articulate why.

*What to watch for:* Declining trust in professional institutions across multiple domains simultaneously. The sense that institutions are "going through the motions" without genuine substance. Inability of institutions to provide convincing accounts of their internal deliberative processes. Growing perception that institutional outputs (decisions, publications, judgments) reflect process rather than genuine wisdom.

---

**28. Generational Discontinuity in Critical Thinking**

Measurable differences emerge between generations in the capacity for certain forms of independent reasoning — sustained analytical reading, multi-step logical argument, productive engagement with complexity — that cannot be fully accounted for by educational differences and appear to correlate with patterns of AI use during formation.

*What to watch for:* Cross-generational differences in performance on tasks requiring sustained independent reasoning. Educational assessors reporting qualitative differences in how younger cohorts engage with complexity. Declining scores on assessments of analytical reasoning across cohorts that have formed primarily in AI-rich environments. The emergence of workarounds — new forms of apparent competence that rely on AI mediation — that mask genuine capability decline.

---

### Critical Markers: Civilisational

**29. Loss of Independent Institutional Knowledge**

Major societal institutions — governments, professional bodies, universities, courts — can no longer independently determine truth in their core domains. They are dependent on AI systems for knowledge they should hold internally, and they have lost the human expertise needed to evaluate whether those AI systems are reliable.

This is the civilisational equivalent of the individual who can no longer form their own views: a society that can no longer collectively think for itself about the questions that matter most.

*What to watch for:* Governments unable to assess AI-generated policy analysis without using AI to do so. Regulatory bodies without the in-house expertise to evaluate the systems they are regulating. Courts relying on AI-generated expert testimony without independent judicial capacity to evaluate it. Universities whose research capacity is substantially dependent on AI systems they cannot fully interrogate.

---

**30. The Ratchet Locks**

The self-reinforcing dynamic identified in the main framework becomes fully operative: the erosion of judgment has proceeded far enough that the capacity to recognise and address the erosion has itself been substantially lost. This is the point at which recovery becomes not merely difficult but genuinely uncertain.

Indicators that this threshold is approaching:
- Public discourse on AI risk and judgment erosion is itself substantially AI-mediated
- The institutions best positioned to address the problem have been among those most affected
- The educational pipeline is producing cohorts that have never experienced what serious independent intellectual culture looks like
- The political will to address the problem cannot be assembled because the deliberative systems needed to assemble it are degraded

---

### Civilisational Monitoring Framework

Unlike the individual and institutional self-assessments, civilisational indicators cannot be captured in a simple checklist — they require ongoing monitoring across multiple domains. The following domains should be tracked:

| Domain | Key Indicator | Monitoring Approach |
|--------|---------------|---------------------|
| Education | Generational capacity for independent analytical reasoning | Longitudinal assessment data; educator reports |
| Media | Diversity and independence of serious information sources | Source count; ownership concentration data |
| Science | Independence and rigour of research verification | Retraction rates; replication study outcomes |
| Law | Quality of independent legal reasoning; human vs. AI judgment in courts | Case analysis; bar examination data |
| Medicine | Independent diagnostic and clinical reasoning capacity | Unaided performance assessments; error correlation studies |
| Democracy | Quality of public deliberation; citizen capacity for policy reasoning | Deliberative polling; political discourse analysis |
| Professional bodies | Maintenance of genuine competence standards | Licensing examination data; independent audit |
| AI concentration | Number of entities controlling primary knowledge mediation | Platform market share; AI training data sourcing |

## Part Four: The Positive Indicators — Signs of Health

Erosion metrics are incomplete without their counterparts: the positive signs that judgment is being actively maintained or recovered. These are worth tracking as deliberately as the warning signs.

### Individual Health Indicators

- **Productive discomfort:** The person regularly engages with ideas and challenges that stretch and resist them — and finds this valuable rather than merely unpleasant.
- **View ownership:** When asked what they think, they can articulate a position with reasons, acknowledge genuine uncertainty, and distinguish their considered view from things they have merely encountered.
- **Verification habit:** They routinely check important claims. This is not anxious or laborious — it is a practised reflex.
- **Formative practice maintenance:** They protect the intellectual, relational, and practical habits that form them, regardless of available AI shortcuts.
- **AI as emissary:** They can clearly articulate, for any given AI interaction, what they brought to the conversation and what the AI contributed — and the former is always genuinely theirs.
- **Calibrated confidence:** They are appropriately confident in areas of genuine expertise and genuinely uncertain where uncertainty is warranted — not uniformly confident and not uniformly deferential.

### Institutional Health Indicators

- **Double competence:** The institution's members can perform core functions both with and without AI assistance. AI improves quality and efficiency; it does not substitute for foundational capacity.
- **Active knowledge transmission:** Experienced practitioners are actively engaged in developing the genuine competence of their successors. Mentorship is relational, not delegated to AI onboarding.
- **Adversarial review culture:** AI-generated outputs are routinely subjected to independent human scrutiny. There is a cultural norm of questioning rather than accepting.
- **Competence testing:** The institution regularly tests independent performance, not just AI-assisted performance, and acts on what it finds.
- **Clear accountability:** When AI is involved in a significant decision, it is clear which human is responsible for that decision and on what basis they made it.

### Civilisational Health Indicators

- **Epistemic diversity:** Multiple independent institutions with genuine human expertise are producing original knowledge, analysis, and evaluation across all major domains.
- **Verification capacity:** Societies maintain robust, well-resourced systems for independently verifying important claims.
- **Broad competence distribution:** Practical and intellectual competence is distributed widely enough that no single system failure would incapacitate entire domains.
- **Generational transmission:** Each generation of practitioners is developed with genuine depth, not merely proficiency in current AI tools.
- **Deliberative quality:** Public and institutional deliberation on important questions engages genuinely with complexity, evidence, and competing values — not merely mobilising pre-formed positions.

## Part Five: Using This Framework

### For Individuals

Run the self-assessment honestly and note the areas of highest score. Don't act on every item simultaneously — that is not sustainable. Instead:

1. Identify the one or two areas of greatest concern
2. Design a specific, small practice change for each
3. Schedule a reassessment in three months
4. Share your commitments with one other person who will ask you about them

The most powerful single intervention for most individuals is protecting one formative practice from AI displacement — writing, deep reading, unaided problem-solving — and maintaining it with deliberate consistency.

### For Institutions

The institutional self-assessment works best when completed independently by multiple people within the institution — at different levels of seniority and different relationships to AI use — and the results compared. Disagreement in the results is itself informative: it may reveal that those closest to the work see a different picture from those in leadership, or that different departments are at very different stages of erosion.

The most powerful institutional intervention is typically assessment reform: changing how the institution measures performance to ensure that AI-mediated output and genuine human competence are genuinely distinguished.

### For Communities and Churches

The framework can be used as a basis for group discernment. Consider running the individual self-assessment as a group exercise, sharing results anonymously, and discussing together:

- What patterns do we see in our community?
- Which indicators are we most concerned about?
- What practices could we commit to together to maintain our collective judgment?
- How do we hold one another accountable to these practices?

The communal dimension is important. Judgment erosion is not just a personal problem. It affects the shared life of communities — their ability to discern together, to hold one another to account, and to transmit their heritage of wisdom to the next generation.

### As an Ongoing Practice

This framework is not a one-time diagnostic. It is a tool for ongoing attention. Some indicators change slowly; others can shift quickly in response to new tools, new pressures, or new institutional decisions.

Consider building a regular review into personal, institutional, and community life — quarterly for individuals, annually for institutions — and tracking movement over time. The direction of movement matters as much as the current position: a score that is improving, even from a low baseline, is a very different situation from one that is declining from a high one.

---

## A Closing Reflection

The metrics in this framework are ultimately in service of a single, simple question:

*Are we still thinking for ourselves?*

Not perfectly. Not without assistance. Not unaided by tools, traditions, and communities. But genuinely — bringing our own minds, our own values, our own sense of responsibility to the questions that matter. Forming views we can own and defend. Exercising the judgment that is ours to exercise.

The answer to that question cannot be fully read from any checklist. It is felt in the quality of our thinking, the depth of our conversations, the honesty of our self-examination, and the care with which we engage the world. The metrics point toward it. The reality lives in us.
