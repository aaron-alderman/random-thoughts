# Sequencing and Pilots
### The bridge from mapped terrain to the first concrete wave of action

---

## Preface: What This Document Does

The rest of this folder lays out the landscape. This document answers the practical question: where do we start if the aim is survival across all timescales, with local and immediate viability as the binding constraint?

The answer is not "solve everything in order." The answer is to build one stack that touches the whole system at small scale, prove it can be lived, and then federate.

---

## I. Sequencing Logic

The correct order is not abstract-to-concrete. It is:

1. stabilize the household
2. form a local cell
3. give the cell one real shared burden
4. add one layer of productive capability
5. add one layer of cultural and truth practice
6. connect the cell outward to one larger federated project

This order matters because people trust what works under pressure. Each step should generate visible local benefit before asking for larger-scale commitment.

---

## II. The First-Wave Prototype: Local Cell Stack

The first pilot should be a local cell of roughly 6-12 adults or 3-8 households with explicit norms and one-year commitments. It should include the following layers.

### 1. Mutual aid and repair norms

- written charter
- meeting cadence
- conflict and forgiveness protocol
- entry and exit expectations

### 2. Food and water backup

- household pantry standard
- pooled reserve and rotation system
- one shared cooking or preservation practice

### 3. Energy backup

- outage communications plan
- charging, lighting, and cooking backup
- tool sharing and one basic repair skill workshop

### 4. Child and family support

- babysitting exchange
- postpartum or illness meal support
- family commons meals

### 5. Practical skill transmission

- recurring workshop in repair, first aid, growing, budgeting, or fabrication
- explicit pairing of more skilled and less skilled members

### 6. Local truth practices

- regular reading or study session
- explicit disagreement norms
- one issue where the group gathers local reality rather than outsourcing judgment

### 7. Communal art and ritual

- shared meal rhythm
- song, craft, story, or liturgical calendar
- visible celebration and mourning practices

### 8. One outward-facing scientific or engineering project

This is the key bridge. The cell must not become a bunker. It needs one project that links local resilience to the larger civilizational horizon.

Examples:

- low-cost environmental monitoring
- energy-use measurement and retrofit work
- amateur astronomy and skywatching with children
- small-scale controlled growing systems
- fabrication or repair work that contributes to a larger technical network

## III. One-Year Pilot Sequence

### Months 1-2: Formation

- identify households or adults with baseline goodwill
- draft charter, cadence, and repair protocol
- choose one real shared burden to solve

### Months 3-4: Household hardening

- pantry and water baseline
- first-aid baseline
- outage baseline
- family support baseline

### Months 5-6: Shared competence

- launch one monthly skill workshop
- begin tool sharing
- create vulnerable-person support map

### Months 7-8: Truth and culture

- start study circle
- start feast or Sabbath rhythm
- test disagreement and repair protocol on a real issue

### Months 9-10: Outward project

- choose one scientific or engineering project with clear measurement
- document progress publicly enough for learning, not for vanity

### Months 11-12: Review and federation

- assess what held and what failed
- revise charter and practices
- decide whether to split, replicate, or federate with another cell

---

## IV. Gating Criteria

The pilot is working only if several things become true at once:

- members are more resilient materially than when they began
- trust has increased rather than collapsed into resentment
- at least one family burden is lighter
- at least one real skill has been transmitted
- the group can tell the truth about failures without disintegrating
- joy is more available, not less
- the outward-facing project has produced a real artifact, dataset, or capability

If the group becomes more ideological, more exhausted, more joyless, or more dependent on one heroic organizer, the pilot is failing.

---

## V. Federation Path

After the first year, the next step is not immediate scale. It is bounded federation.

Cells should connect around a few shared functions:

- reserve sharing
- training exchange
- conflict and charter learning
- common reporting on local indicators
- one larger technical or resilience project

The federation should remain protocol-based, not culture-uniform. What must be shared is method, interface, and honesty, not a single total worldview.

---

## VI. Suggested First Pilot Set

If starting from zero, the highest-leverage first set is:

1. `family commons`
2. `pantry and water baseline`
3. `outage and first-aid baseline`
4. `monthly repair or skill night`
5. `weekly truth circle`
6. `monthly feast with music or art`
7. `one measurement-based engineering or science project`

That set is small enough to attempt and broad enough to test the theory.

---

## VII. Immediate Output Standard

Any future iteration of this folder should improve one of three things:

- sharper local prototypes
- clearer federation protocols
- stronger link from local resilience to frontier capability

If an iteration becomes more rhetorically grand but less executable by a real local cell, it is moving in the wrong direction.
