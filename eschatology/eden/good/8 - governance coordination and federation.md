# Governance, Coordination, and Federation
### Shared action at scale without flattening the remainder that keeps systems honest

---

## 1. Current Failure Pattern

Governance failures show up in two forms that reinforce one another:

- abstract central systems that are too far away to be trusted
- fragmented local systems that cannot hold against defection pressure

The result is stalemate: too much administrative distance for loyalty, too little binding structure for durable cooperation.

## 2. Why This Matters Across All Timescales

- immediately, weak local governance leaves families and neighborhoods alone with system-level problems
- regionally and nationally, low-trust coordination prevents commons management
- civilizationally, the surplus problem cannot be solved without institutions that preserve local remainder while enabling shared action

This domain is the hinge. If it fails, the rest remain scattered efforts.

## 3. Minimum Viable Local Condition

A local cell needs explicit norms and real stakes.

Minimum conditions:

- clear entry, commitment, and exit rules
- conflict and repair protocols
- role clarity for meetings, money, and projects
- one shared problem that requires real cooperation

## 4. Scale-by-Scale Interventions

### Household

- `0-2 years`: weekly household council, explicit money and care norms
- `3-10 years`: stronger routines for major decisions and conflict repair
- `10-50+ years`: inherited household governance that teaches responsibility

### Local cell/community

- `0-2 years`: written charter, cadence, conflict process, and shared project
- `3-10 years`: federate multiple cells around specific common goods
- `10-50+ years`: local institutions that can hand off leadership without collapse

### City/bioregion

- `0-2 years`: district forums tied to concrete issues like water, transport, or safety
- `3-10 years`: subsidiarity structures that let local knowledge correct city policy
- `10-50+ years`: real municipal federalism rather than symbolic consultation

### Nation-state

- `0-2 years`: identify policy areas that should be pushed downward or pulled upward
- `3-10 years`: renew federal design around competence and subsidiarity
- `10-50+ years`: state capacity strong enough for essentials, restrained enough to preserve plural forms

### Regional bloc

- `0-2 years`: coordinate around limited shared functions
- `3-10 years`: treaty-based governance for commons and infrastructure
- `10-50+ years`: federated blocs that remain functional without becoming imperial abstractions

### International/civilizational

- `0-2 years`: protocol consortia for shared risks
- `3-10 years`: auditing and standards where the territory can arbitrate
- `10-50+ years`: civilizational coordination through interoperable protocols, not world government

### Spacefaring species

- `0-2 years`: draft settlement charter concepts early
- `3-10 years`: clarify autonomy, interoperability, and common law between frontier nodes
- `10-50+ years`: multi-planet federation built from protocol and shared stakes rather than conquest

## 5. Practical Bottleneck

The bottleneck is the missing middle. Many people can imagine family and nation, but not the intermediate federated forms that make scale humane and durable.

## 7. Lead Indicators

- number of local groups with explicit charters and repair paths
- participation rates in concrete local decision forums
- time required to resolve conflicts and defections
- public trust in local versus remote institutions
- number of functioning federated projects across multiple cells

## 8. Failure Modes

- localism becomes factionalism
- federal language hides power hoarding
- protocol systems become technocratic and illegible
- institutions punish error so hard that no one risks honest participation

## 9. Open Questions

- which local stakes are strong enough to bind cooperation now?
- what is the smallest viable charter for a local cell?
- how can regional coordination stay strong without drifting into fiat administration?

## 10. Candidate Pilot Programs

- a 6-12 person cell with written norms, conflict protocol, and one real shared project
- a district-level commons council for one issue such as food, water, or outage readiness
- a federated charter template that multiple local groups can adapt without standardizing culture
